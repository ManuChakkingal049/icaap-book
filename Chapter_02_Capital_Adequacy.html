2.1 — The Story

The Sponge Cake School of Capital Management

A Beginner's Parable

Imagine a bank is like a very expensive sponge cake at a wedding. The cake (total assets) is magnificent — six layers, fondant icing, the works. But underneath all that icing are three distinct layers of cake.

The bottom layer is pure, dense, high-quality chocolate sponge — the best stuff. This is CET1 — ordinary share capital and retained earnings. It absorbs everything first. If a guest drops the cake, this layer takes the hit. The middle layer is good vanilla sponge, but slightly drier — this is Additional Tier 1, bonds that convert to equity under stress. The top decorative layer is a thinner lemon sponge — Tier 2 capital, subordinated debt. Nice to have, but the last thing you want to be relying on when the cake hits the floor.

Now here is the punchline: the regulator comes to the wedding and says "your cake must be at least 8% cake and not too much fancy icing (assets)." That is the capital ratio minimum. But your honest internal assessment — ICAAP — says "given the buffet table is unstable, the venue has three large dogs, and Uncle Ahmed is already eyeing the cake, we should probably make 12% of this thing solid sponge, not 8%." The capital stack is the recipe. Capital ratios measure how much solid cake you have per unit of wedding risk.

Quality decreases downward. CET1 is the only capital that truly absorbs losses without conditions. Everything else has a catch.
2.2 — Why They Exist

Why Capital Ratios Exist — The Simple and Technical Answer

🟢 Simple Version — The Skin in the Game Principle

Banks borrow most of what they lend. A bank with AED 100 in assets might only have AED 10 of its own money (equity) and AED 90 borrowed from depositors. If AED 11 of loans go bad, the bank cannot repay its depositors. Capital ratios measure how thick that equity cushion is. The thicker it is, the more losses the bank can absorb before depositors start losing their money.

Technical explanation: Banks operate on fractional reserve principles — a small amount of equity supports a large balance sheet. This leverage amplifies returns in good times but creates systemic fragility in bad times. Capital adequacy regulation exists to ensure the equity cushion is sufficiently large that:

  • Unexpected losses can be absorbed without triggering insolvency
  • Depositors and senior creditors are protected from bank failures
  • Systemic contagion is limited — one bank's failure does not immediately bankrupt another
  • Banks have a private incentive to limit risk-taking (they lose their own equity first)

Capital ratios are expressed as a percentage of Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA) rather than total assets — because not all assets carry the same risk. A government bond and an unsecured personal loan both sit on the balance sheet at face value, but the loan is far more likely to default. RWA adjusts for this difference.

The Core Capital Ratio Logic
Capital Ratio = Qualifying Capital ÷ Risk-Weighted Assets × 100 The higher this ratio, the more capital per unit of risk. Regulatory minimum: the floor below which a bank is non-compliant. ICAAP target: the level the bank determines it needs above the floor.
RWA = each asset × its prescribed risk weight. A UAE sovereign bond: 0% weight. An unsecured consumer loan: 75–150% weight.

Why Regulators Focus on CET1 Above Everything

CET1 is the only capital that absorbs losses unconditionally. AT1 instruments have triggers — they only convert to equity when CET1 falls below a threshold. Tier 2 only absorbs in resolution — by which point depositors may already be harmed. In an ICAAP stress test, regulators almost exclusively focus on whether CET1 stays above 7% throughout the stress horizon. The other ratios matter — but CET1 is the number that determines whether the bank survives.

2.4 — CET1 Ratio

CET1 Ratio — Deep Dive

CET1 Ratio Formula
CET1 Ratio = CET1 Capital ÷ Risk-Weighted Assets (Pillar 1) × 100 CET1 Capital = Paid-up ordinary shares + Share premium (from ordinary shares) + Retained earnings + Accumulated other comprehensive income (AOCI) + Other disclosed reserves - Regulatory deductions (goodwill, deferred tax, IRB shortfall etc.)
Sources: Basel III, December 2010 (BIS), Art. 52–88. CBUAE Capital Standards 2018, Section 2.

2.5 — Tier 1 & Total Capital

Tier 1 and Total Capital Ratio

Tier 1 and Total Capital Formulas
Tier 1 Ratio = (CET1 + AT1 Capital) ÷ RWA × 100 [Min: 6.0%] Total Capital = (Tier 1 + Tier 2 Capital) ÷ RWA × 100 [Min: 8.0%]
AT1 instruments: must be perpetual, fully paid, subordinated, non-cumulative discretionary distributions. Tier 2: subordinated debt with original maturity ≥ 5 years; amortised in final 5 years before maturity.
🟢 When Does Tier 1 vs CET1 Matter?

Most analysts focus almost exclusively on CET1 because AT1 instruments have conversion triggers — they only help when CET1 is already severely depleted. In a going-concern stress scenario (the bank is hurt but alive), AT1 may not have triggered yet. The Tier 1 ratio matters most for resolution planning — how much total loss-absorbing capacity the bank has in a wind-down. In ICAAP, CET1 is always the binding constraint.

2.6 — Leverage Ratio

The Leverage Ratio — The Non-Risk-Based Backstop

Leverage Ratio Formula
Leverage Ratio = Tier 1 Capital ÷ Total Exposure Measure × 100 [Min: 3.0%] Total Exposure = On-balance sheet assets (at exposure value) + Off-balance sheet commitments (at CCF) + Derivatives (replacement cost + PFE add-on) + Securities financing transactions
Source: BCBS, Basel III leverage ratio framework, January 2014 (rev. 2016). BIS. CBUAE: minimum 3%, consistent with Basel III.
🟢 Why the Leverage Ratio Exists — The Simple Story

Risk-weighted capital ratios can be gamed. A bank can hold enormous volumes of "low risk weight" assets — government bonds at 0%, covered bonds at 10% — and appear very well capitalised by CET1 ratio while actually being dangerously leveraged. The leverage ratio ignores risk weights entirely. It simply says: "For every AED 33 of assets you hold, you must have at least AED 1 of Tier 1 capital." It is a blunt instrument — but blunt instruments are hard to game.

⚠️
The Pre-2008 Banks That Failed the Leverage Test

Several large European banks failed in 2008 with reported CET1 ratios above 8% — because their internal models assigned very low risk weights to mortgage-backed securities and sovereign debt. Their actual leverage ratios were sometimes 40:1 or higher. The leverage ratio was introduced specifically to prevent this. Under the leverage ratio, those positions would have been captured at full face value regardless of internal models.

RWA — The Denominator That Changes Everything

💡
Pillar 2 Does NOT Change the Published CET1 Ratio

Pillar 2 capital requirements operate on the numerator threshold — they raise the minimum the ratio must exceed. They do NOT add to the RWA denominator in the published ratio. A Pillar 2 requirement of 2% means the bank's CET1 ratio (calculated on Pillar 1 RWA) must be at least 9% (7% floor + 2% P2R) — not that the denominator gets bigger. However, in ICAAP stress testing, the bank builds a larger stressed RWA that includes Pillar 2 risk add-ons for internal capital planning purposes.

2.8 — Provisions & Tier 2

Provisions, the Balance Sheet, and Why General Provisions Qualify for Tier 2

This is one of the most conceptually challenging areas of bank capital accounting — and one of the most frequently misunderstood by practitioners. The key question is: if a provision reduces both the asset (contra asset) and equity (via P&L), how can it also count as Tier 2 capital?

🟢 The Insurance Premium Analogy

Think of a general provision like an insurance premium. When you pay a premium, your bank balance goes down (equity reduced) — but now you hold an insurance policy (the provision sits as a contra asset and then as a collective allowance). The policy has real value — it will pay out if a claim arises. The premium cost and the policy value are two different things. You paid for the protection. The protection itself still exists and has value — that is what Tier 2 recognises.

The Accounting — What Actually Happens on the Balance Sheet

When a general provision is raised (AED 50m example):

Journal Entry — General Provision
Dr: Impairment expense (P&L income statement) ............ 50 Cr: Loan loss allowance (contra asset, balance sheet) ...... 50 Effect on balance sheet: → Gross loans: UNCHANGED at face value (still 1,000) → Loan loss allowance: NEW entry of (50) on ASSET side → Net loans: 950 (gross 1,000 minus allowance 50) → Retained earnings: FALLS by 50 (via P&L charge) → Liabilities: UNTOUCHED — they are not involved → Total assets fall 50. Total equity falls 50. Balance sheet balances.
The contra asset and equity reduction are two permanent, simultaneous entries from one journal entry. Neither disappears until the loan is written off.

Why This Does NOT Make Tier 2 Double-Counting

The provision already reduced CET1 through the P&L hit. That is acknowledged. But the loan loss allowance now sits on the balance sheet as a funded buffer — a pre-paid pool of loss absorption — that will absorb future write-offs before they hit equity again. When a loan is subsequently written off against the allowance:

Write-Off Entry (later, when loan finally defaults)
Dr: Loan loss allowance ............ 50 (removes the contra asset) Cr: Gross loans ..................... 50 (removes the gross loan) Effect: Both sides of the asset shrink by 50. Equity is NOT touched. The P&L pain was taken at provision. Write-off is just tidying the balance sheet.
This is why adequate provisioning is critical — if provisions are sufficient, write-offs are smooth. If under-provisioned, write-offs require a fresh P&L charge, creating a sudden CET1 drop.

💡
The IRB Shortfall Deduction — A Hidden CET1 Risk

Under the Internal Ratings-Based approach, the bank compares its actual provisions to its modelled Expected Loss (EL). If actual provisions are LESS than modelled EL, the entire shortfall is deducted from CET1. This is a double hit: the bank has under-provisioned (bad for credit quality ratios) AND has its CET1 further reduced by the shortfall amount. UAE banks using IRB models must monitor this carefully — an unexpected EL model revision can instantly reduce CET1 by hundreds of millions.

2.9 — UAE Context (Standalone)
🇦🇪 UAE Specific — Standalone Section

Are Basel Capital Ratios Directly Suitable for UAE Banks?

The Basel capital ratio framework is broadly applicable to UAE banks — the CBUAE has adopted the CET1, Tier 1, and Total Capital ratio requirements closely aligned with Basel III. However, four UAE-specific adaptations affect how capital ratios are interpreted and applied.

Adaptation 1 — CBUAE Capital Standards and Minimum Levels

The CBUAE Capital Adequacy Standards (Circular 163/2018, updated 2021) set UAE-specific minimums consistent with Basel III: CET1 minimum 4.5%, Capital Conservation Buffer 2.5%, Total effective CET1 floor 7.0%. The CBUAE has not yet activated the Countercyclical Buffer (CCyB) in the UAE as of 2024, though it retains the power to do so.[2]

Adaptation 2 — D-SIB Surcharges

The CBUAE identified five Domestic Systemically Important Banks in 2020: First Abu Dhabi Bank (FAB), Emirates NBD (ENBD), Abu Dhabi Commercial Bank (ADCB), Dubai Islamic Bank (DIB), and Mashreq. These banks face additional CET1 surcharges of 0.5–2.5%, raising their effective CET1 floor above 7%. This directly affects their ICAAP internal capital targets.[4]

Adaptation 3 — Islamic Capital Instruments

UAE Islamic banks issue AT1 and Tier 2 capital instruments structured as Islamic contracts (Mudaraba or Musharaka sukuk) rather than conventional bonds. The CBUAE Standards for Islamic banks (2018) govern eligibility criteria — these instruments must be perpetual, non-cumulative, and loss-absorbing in a manner consistent with Sharia.[6] The capital ratio formula is identical — only the composition of qualifying instruments differs.

Adaptation 4 — RWA Treatment of UAE Government Exposures

Under the Basel Standardised Approach, claims on UAE federal and emirate-level governments rated AA- and above receive 0% risk weight. However, GRE exposures in the UAE — a large component of many UAE banks' loan books — receive higher risk weights (typically 20–100% depending on rating and whether guaranteed). This means UAE banks with high GRE exposure have materially higher credit RWA than a simple government exposure analysis would suggest.

UAE-Specific ICAAP Capital Drivers

Three Pillar 2 risks dominate UAE banks' ICAAP capital add-ons and are not adequately captured by Pillar 1 formulas: (1) Real estate concentration — property cycles in Dubai and Abu Dhabi create correlated losses that standardised risk weights underestimate; (2) GRE single-name concentration — several UAE banks have individual GRE exposures exceeding 10% of Tier 1 capital; (3) Funding concentration — government and GRE deposits represent unstable funding that requires higher capital buffers under stress scenarios calibrated to the 2008–2009 Dubai crisis.

2.10 — Window Dressing

How Window Dressers Hide — Capital Ratios

🎭
How Window Dressers Hide in Capital Ratios
Four techniques that inflate reported CET1 without genuinely improving the bank's loss-absorption capacity — and the footprints each leaves behind

Capital ratio window dressing operates by making the reported CET1 ratio appear stronger than the bank's genuine loss-absorption capacity. Every technique either inflates the numerator artificially or deflates the denominator artificially. The techniques are technically within the rules — which is what makes them dangerous and why detection requires cross-ratio analysis rather than simply reading the reported number.

1
IRB Model Recalibration — Shrinking the Denominator Without Reducing Risk

How They Hide: Under the Internal Ratings-Based approach, banks use internal models to assign Probability of Default (PD) and Loss Given Default (LGD) to loan portfolios. By recalibrating these models downward — even slightly — RWA falls, and the CET1 ratio rises mechanically. The loan book is unchanged. The risk is unchanged. Only the model's measurement of it has changed.

Numerical Example:

Bank reports CET1 capital: AED 3,360m (unchanged) Original RWA (pre-recalibration): AED 28,000m Original CET1 ratio: 12.0% Model recalibration: LGD on residential mortgages revised from 28% to 21% (Bank argues recent low default data supports lower LGD — technically defensible) New RWA after recalibration: AED 24,640m (↓ 12%) New reported CET1 ratio: 3,360 ÷ 24,640 = 13.6% ← up 160bp Capital "improvement": +160bp with ZERO change in actual risk or capital held Basel IV output floor: minimum RWA = 72.5% × standardised approach RWA Standardised RWA (parallel calc): AED 34,000m Output floor RWA: AED 24,650m → floor is binding — no gaming possible (Basel IV output floor effectively closes this loophole from 2025 onwards)

Footprint & Detection: Monitor RWA density = RWA ÷ total assets over time. A bank with an unchanged loan book but falling RWA density is recalibrating models. Compare IRB risk weights against the standardised approach equivalent. Request a parallel standardised calculation. The Basel IV output floor (72.5% of SA RWA minimum) was specifically designed to limit this technique from 2025 implementation.

2
Dividend Suspension — Temporary CET1 Boost Without Business Improvement

How They Hide: Suspending dividends retains earnings in CET1. The ratio improves. But the bank's underlying earnings engine — PPP/RWA — is unchanged. The moment dividends resume, CET1 growth returns to its pre-suspension trajectory. This is a temporary deferral, not a genuine capital improvement.

Numerical Example:

Bank earnings: AED 480m net profit p.a. Normal payout ratio: 70% → Dividend = AED 336m | Retained = AED 144m Normal CET1 growth: 144 ÷ 28,000 RWA = +0.51% per year Window dressing: Bank suspends dividend for 1 year New retained earnings: AED 480m (full profit retained) CET1 boost vs normal: +336m ÷ 28,000 = +1.20% vs normal year 1-year CET1 improvement from suspension: +120bp (looks dramatic) Sustainable recurring improvement: ZERO (payout ratio will return to 70%) PPP/RWA: UNCHANGED at 0.6% — the earnings quality problem remains

Footprint & Detection: Track the payout ratio trend. A sudden drop from 70% to 0% is not a business improvement. Calculate "core CET1 generation" = retained earnings ÷ RWA at the normalised payout ratio. If a bank suspends dividends for 2+ years, ask whether it plans to resume and what happens to CET1 trajectory when it does. Model the "dividend catch-up" scenario.

3
One-Time Disposal Gains — Inflating the Numerator With Non-Recurring Profit

How They Hide: Selling investment securities or properties at a gain books a one-time profit that flows through to retained earnings and CET1. The ratio looks dramatically improved. But this improvement is non-recurring — the bank cannot sell the same asset twice. Recurring PPP/RWA is unchanged, meaning the bank's capacity to sustain capital in future periods is no better than before.

Numerical Example:

Starting CET1: AED 3,360m (12.0% of RWA 28,000m) Recurring annual PPP/RWA: 0.6% → pre-provision profit = AED 168m p.a. Window dressing: Bank sells sovereign bond portfolio purchased in 2018 at higher yield; now trading at premium due to rate decline. Gain on sale: AED 185m (pre-tax) Tax at 9%: AED 17m Post-tax gain: AED 168m → added to retained earnings New CET1: 3,360 + 168 = AED 3,528m New CET1 ratio: 3,528 ÷ 28,000 = 12.6% ← up 60bp Looks like improvement. But: Core (recurring) CET1 generation: still 0.6% PPP/RWA → still below cost of risk The bond portfolio that generated the gain is now GONE Future NIM reduced as replacement portfolio bought at lower yield Net sustainable position: WORSE than before the disposal

Footprint & Detection: Separate recurring from non-recurring income in the income statement. Calculate "core PPP/RWA" excluding disposal gains, insurance settlements, and revaluation credits. A CET1 improvement of >50bp in a single quarter driven entirely by disposal gains without any improvement in core earnings is a clear flag. Also check: what replaced the sold asset on the balance sheet?

4
Off-Balance Sheet Structuring — Cosmetic RWA Reduction With Retained Economic Risk

How They Hide: Moving assets off the balance sheet through securitisation or synthetic risk transfer reduces RWA and improves the CET1 ratio. If the bank genuinely transfers the economic risk to investors, this is legitimate capital relief. If the bank retains the economic risk through guarantees, first-loss retained tranches, or put options — the capital relief is illusory while the ratio improvement is real.

Numerical Example:

Bank securitises AED 800m mortgage portfolio: — Sells 85% senior tranche (AED 680m) to investors: genuine transfer — Retains 15% first-loss piece (AED 120m): economic risk retained Accounting treatment (if derecognised): RWA removed: 800m × 65% avg weight = 520m RWA off balance sheet CET1 ratio improvement: 520 ÷ 28,000 = +1.9% on CET1 ratio But economic reality: First-loss piece absorbs first AED 120m of losses on the ENTIRE AED 800m pool Effective concentration risk retained: 15% × 800m = AED 120m at 1250% risk weight Regulatory RWA for first-loss: 120m × 1250% = AED 1,500m → much HIGHER than before CET1 ratio with correct regulatory treatment: 3,360 ÷ (28,000 + 980) = 11.6% ← LOWER Hidden: AED 380m of CET1 impact from incorrect derecognition treatment

Footprint & Detection: Review off-balance sheet commitments note and securitisation disclosures. Identify retained interests in securitised pools. A bank whose off-balance sheet exposure grows rapidly while on-balance sheet shrinks, but total credit commitments (including off-B/S) remain stable, is likely structuring for capital relief. Apply the "substance over form" test: who bears the first loss?

2.11 — Detection
🔍 Be Sherlock Holmes — Detecting Capital Ratio Window Dressing
"It is a capital mistake to theorise before one has data." The data is always there — you just have to know where to look.
1

The RWA Density Clue: Calculate RWA ÷ total assets (RWA density) every quarter for 8 quarters. It should be broadly stable for an unchanged portfolio mix. A falling RWA density with no documented change in portfolio composition is the primary signal of IRB model recalibration gaming.

Test: RWA density = RWA ÷ Total Assets Healthy range for UAE mixed bank: 55–75% Red flag: Density falls more than 5pp in 4 quarters with no major portfolio mix change If density falls from 68% to 59%: potential RWA understatement of 8,000 × 9% = AED 720m Apply Basel IV output floor: RWA must be ≥ 72.5% × SA-RWA. Request SA parallel run.

2

The Payout Ratio Clue: Track dividend payout ratio (dividends paid ÷ net profit) over 5 years. A sudden decline to 0% is not an organic improvement — it is capital conservation. Model "normalised CET1 trajectory" = what CET1 would have been at the historical 5-year average payout ratio. Does the bank still meet its target on this normalised basis?

Example: Bank's 5-year average payout = 65%. Current year: 0%. At 65% payout on AED 480m profit: CET1 would have grown by only AED 168m At 0% payout: CET1 grew by AED 480m "Artificial" CET1 boost from suspension: AED 312m = 1.1% of CET1 ratio Ask: When dividends resume at 65%, how does CET1 trajectory look for 3 years?

3

The Core Earnings Separation Clue: Decompose reported net profit into: (a) recurring net interest income and fee income, (b) disposal gains and revaluation credits, (c) provision releases. Calculate CET1 trajectory using only (a). If CET1 growth depends heavily on (b) and (c), the bank's capital position is deteriorating in recurring terms even if the reported ratio is stable.

Red flag: "Non-core income as % of net profit" > 25% for 2+ consecutive years Test formula: Core CET1 growth = (NII + fee income − opex − tax − normalised impairment) × (1 − payout) ÷ RWA If core CET1 growth < 0: bank is consuming capital in recurring terms despite stable reported ratio

4

The Off-Balance Sheet Clue: Compare growth in total credit commitments (on + off balance sheet) against growth in reported RWA. If commitments grow 15% but RWA grows only 5%, credit risk has moved off-balance sheet. Request a breakdown of the RWA impact of all off-balance sheet exposures. Check that first-loss retained interests in securitisations are capitalised at 1250% risk weight as required under Basel III.

Test: Total credit exposure ratio = (loans + off-B/S credit commitments) ÷ RWA This should be broadly stable. If it rises from 2.1× to 2.8×, off-B/S exposure is growing faster than RWA captures. For each securitisation: identify the size and risk weight of retained first-loss piece. Correct capital impact = first-loss size × 1250% risk weight.

5

The Provision Shortfall Clue (IRB Banks): Request the ECL model Expected Loss (EL) calculation alongside reported provisions. If actual provisions are materially below EL, the IRB shortfall deduction should be reducing CET1. A bank that uses IRB models but does not disclose whether its provisions exceed or fall short of modelled EL is hiding a potential CET1 headwind.

Test: IRB shortfall = max(0, EL − eligible provisions) This amount is deducted DIRECTLY from CET1 (50% from CET1, 50% from Tier 2 under Basel II; 100% from CET1 under Basel III) If a bank's EL is AED 900m but provisions are AED 650m: shortfall = AED 250m CET1 deduction = AED 250m → equivalent to ~90bp on a AED 28bn RWA bank This shortfall should appear in the Pillar 3 disclosure — check "regulatory capital reconciliation" note.

🚨
Year 3 Breach — What ICAAP Must Address

The stressed projection shows CET1 breaching the 7% regulatory floor in Year 3 by 60bp (= AED 300m additional capital needed). A credible ICAAP must: (1) document this breach explicitly, (2) present specific management actions to prevent it — dividend suspension from Year 1, RWA reduction programme, potential equity issuance in Year 2 if market conditions permit, (3) demonstrate each action is credible and executable, (4) show the revised CET1 trajectory with management actions applied. Hiding this breach or using implausible management actions is the primary form of ICAAP window dressing.
