    The Immaculate Crime Scene

    A Beginner's Parable

    Imagine a detective called to a house where nothing appears to be wrong. The door is locked from the inside. No broken windows. No signs of struggle. The valuables are all in place. The family says everything is fine. By every visible indicator, this is not a crime scene.

    But the detective notices three things. The ornamental vase has been moved slightly — there is a dust ring where it used to stand. The kitchen calendar shows last Tuesday circled, but the family claims they were away that week. And the lock on the back door, while engaged, shows fresh scratch marks on the cylinder as if it was recently re-keyed.

    Individually, each observation is meaningless. Together, they tell a story: someone was in this house last Tuesday, moved the vase, and changed the lock afterwards. The crime was not in what was visible — it was in the inconsistency between things that should have been consistent.

    Window dressing detection in banking is exactly this. The ICAAP document looks immaculate. Every ratio passes. Every narrative is coherent. The crime is in the dust ring around the moved vase — the NPL that is stable while Stage 2 is tripling; the CET1 that improved in a quarter where earnings were negative; the HQLA that grew on exactly March 31st and vanished by April 3rd.

    Window dressing always leaves a crime scene. The ICAAP document is the immaculate house. The detective finds the dust ring.
    7.2 — The Anatomy

    The Anatomy of Window Dressing — What It Is and Is Not

    🟢 Simple: The Three Types of ICAAP Problems

    Type 1 — Genuine weakness: The bank's ratios are truly poor and management is honestly disclosing that in the ICAAP. This is good ICAAP practice — uncomfortable to read, but credible. Type 2 — Innocent error: Misapplication of methodology, outdated data, or genuine model limitations. Fix it. Type 3 — Window dressing: Deliberate construction of an ICAAP narrative to present a passing picture while concealing genuine vulnerabilities. This is the detection target of this chapter.

    The Taxonomy of Window Dressing by Risk Type
    CREDIT QUALITY window dressing: Stage 2 accumulation → flat NPL (Flag 1) Coverage inflation → weak specific provisions masked by general LTV staleness → collateral value overstated Cost of risk smoothing → provision releases inflate earnings CAPITAL window dressing: IRB model recalibration → RWA shrinks without portfolio change Disposal gains → one-time CET1 boost, recurring weakness unchanged Dividend suspension → temporary retention, not structural improvement Off-B/S structuring → cosmetic RWA relief with retained economic risk LIQUIDITY window dressing: Period-end repo → quarter-end ELAR/LCR spike Numerator suppression → loans reclassified out of ASRR advances GRE misclassification → unstable deposits treated as stable Asset overstatement → ineligible assets in ELAR pool PROFITABILITY window dressing: Disposal gain income → non-recurring PPP inflation Leverage compensation → stable ROE hides ROA decline Provision release → loss turned to profit Fee front-loading → current period income stolen from future periods ICAAP PROCESS window dressing: Scenario selection → severity calibrated to always pass Management action fantasy → timeline and feasibility not credible Missing risk add-ons → concentration, IRRBB quantified too low Circular target → ICG reverse-engineered from current CET1
    Total: 20 distinct window dressing techniques catalogued across this book. Each leaves at least one detectable footprint.
    7.3 — Technique 1

    Detection Technique 1 — Cross-Ratio Inconsistency Screening

    🔗
    Cross-Ratio Inconsistency — Finding the Relocated Risk
    Risk cannot be destroyed. Where it left, it must have gone. These are the pairings to test.

    How Window Dressers Create Inconsistency: Every window dressing technique that improves Ratio A must, by necessity, either worsen Ratio B (in a different risk category) or worsen a sub-component of Ratio A itself. The inconsistency is the footprint.

    CROSS-RATIO INCONSISTENCY MATRIX — 12 CRITICAL PAIRINGS Pairing | If this improves... | Check this for deterioration NPL ratio vs Stage 2 | NPL flat/falling | Stage 2 should also be flat/falling Broad vs narrow coverage | Broad rising | Narrow should move proportionately LCR vs NSFR | LCR rising | NSFR should also be rising ELAR vs ASRR | ELAR passing | ASRR should also be passing CET1 ratio vs RWA density | CET1 rising | RWA density should be stable or rising ROE vs ROA | ROE stable | ROA should be stable or rising PPP/RWA vs disposal income | PPP/RWA reported | Core PPP/RWA should be close NIM vs ROE | ROE stable | NIM should not be falling Cost of risk vs Stage 2 trend | CoR smooth | Should correlate with credit quality ICAAP ICG vs current CET1 | ICG reasonable | Should not exactly equal current CET1 Stressed CET1 vs scenario sev. | Stressed CET1 ok | Scenario should be genuinely severe Management actions vs market | Actions restore CET1| Actions must be executable in stress SCORING: Each inconsistency = 1 point. Score >= 4: Investigate. Score >= 7: Escalate.

    Numerical Application — UAE Bank Example:

    Bank X reported metrics (Q4 2024): NPL: 4.2% (stable 6 quarters) → Stage 2: 21.0% (rose 13pp in 6Q) ← INCONSISTENT Broad coverage: 122% → Narrow coverage: 52% ← INCONSISTENT (gap 70pp) ELAR: 16.4% (passing 10% min) → ASRR: 112% (failing 100% max) ← INCONSISTENT CET1: 13.5% (above target) → PPP/RWA: 0.7% (very weak) ← INCONSISTENT ROE: 11.2% (appears adequate) → ROA: 0.71% + leverage multiplier rising ← INCONSISTENT Reported PPP/RWA: 0.7% → Core PPP/RWA (ex disposal): 0.4% ← INCONSISTENT ELAR Q-end: 16.4% → ELAR 90-day avg: 9.8% (below minimum!) ← INCONSISTENT Inconsistency score: 7/12 → ESCALATE for detailed investigation
    7.4 — Technique 2

    Detection Technique 2 — Trend Divergence Analysis

    📈
    Trend Divergence — When the Direction of Change Contradicts
    Healthy banks' ratios respond to the same economic forces in consistent directions. When they do not — something is being managed.

    How Window Dressers Create Trend Divergence: Economic conditions affect all ratios simultaneously. In a deteriorating credit environment, NPL and Stage 2 should both rise; cost of risk should rise; coverage should be consumed; CET1 should fall. A bank where only some ratios respond to economic conditions while others remain artificially stable has someone actively managing the non-responding ratios.

    TREND CONSISTENCY TEST — Apply to 8-Quarter Dataset For each ratio pair, test: do they move in the same direction in the same quarter? Step 1: Identify economic regime (improving / stable / deteriorating) per quarter Step 2: For each ratio, note direction of change (up / stable / down) Step 3: Flag any ratio that moves opposite to what the economic regime predicts Expected in deteriorating environment: NPL: ↑ | Stage 2: ↑ | Cost of risk: ↑ | Coverage: ↓ | NIM: ↓ | CET1: ↓ Window dressing signal: NPL: stable ← MANAGED while Stage 2: ↑↑ — inconsistency confirmed Coverage (broad): ↑ ← INFLATED while narrow: ↓ — composition manipulation confirmed CET1: stable ← MAINTAINED via disposal/IRB while PPP/RWA: ↓↓ — endowment not engine ROE: stable ← COMPENSATED while ROA: ↓, leverage: ↑ — treadmill confirmed Each "goes opposite to expected direction" = 1 trend divergence score Score >= 3: Investigate. Score >= 5: Escalate with supporting evidence.

    7.5 — Technique 3

    Detection Technique 3 — Sub-Ratio Drilling

    🔬
    Sub-Ratio Drilling — Decomposing Headline Ratios Into Their Components
    Every headline ratio is an average of components. Window dressing concentrates the manipulation in one component while leaving others unmolested.

    The Decomposition Principle: No window dresser can manipulate all components of a ratio simultaneously — there are too many interconnections. The technique is to find the component that should have changed but did not, or the component that moved in a way inconsistent with the others.

    SUB-RATIO DRILLING GUIDE — By Headline Ratio NPL RATIO → Drill to: (a) New NPL inflow rate (new Stage 3 ÷ opening Stage 2) — should correlate with Stage 2 level (b) Write-off rate (write-offs ÷ opening NPL) — high write-offs mask incoming NPL inflows (c) Forbearance ratio (forborne ÷ gross loans) — high forbearance = evergreening suspected (d) Stage 2 provision rate vs historical migration LGD — underprovision? COVERAGE RATIO → Drill to: (a) Narrow = specific ÷ NPL only (b) Stage 2 ECL rate = Stage 2 provisions ÷ Stage 2 balance (should be 5–15%) (c) Stage 1 ECL rate = Stage 1 provisions ÷ Stage 1 balance (should be 0.2–1.5%) LCR → Drill to: (a) Level 1 HQLA as % of total HQLA (should be >80%) (b) Day-31 survival: (HQLA − 30-day outflows) ÷ daily wholesale maturity (c) Currency mismatch: USD HQLA ÷ USD outflows CET1 → Drill to: (a) RWA density = RWA ÷ total assets (should be broadly stable) (b) CET1 source split: organic vs capital raise vs disposal gain (c) IRB shortfall = max(0, EL − provisions) — deducted from CET1? ROE → Drill to: (a) ROA = net profit ÷ avg assets (pure earning power) (b) Equity multiplier = assets ÷ equity (leverage) (c) Core PPP/RWA = (NII + recurring fees − opex) ÷ RWA

    Application to Bank X:

    NPL drill for Bank X: Reported NPL: 4.2% (stable) New NPL inflow rate Q4: 18% of opening Stage 2 → should produce NPL rise of ~2.5pp Actual NPL rise: 0.0% ← IMPOSSIBLE without massive write-offs or curing Write-off rate: 2.1% of NPL balance → insufficient to offset inflow rate Forbearance ratio: 8.3% ← HIGH — evergreening suspected CONCLUSION: NPL ratio stability is arithmetically impossible without Stage 2 management Coverage drill for Bank X: Broad coverage: 122% Narrow coverage: 52% ← 70pp gap — massive general provision cushioning Stage 2 ECL rate: 3.2% ← LOW vs historical migration LGD of 35% Implied Stage 2 under-provision: 3,200×(35%−3.2%)×35%migration = AED 357m CET1 drill for Bank X: Reported CET1: 13.5% RWA density Q4: 58% ← fell from 68% 4 quarters ago No portfolio mix change documented Estimated RWA if density unchanged: 28,000 × 68/58 = AED 32,800m Corrected CET1 at unchanged density: 4,200 ÷ 32,800 = 12.8% (vs reported 13.5%)
    7.6 — Technique 4

    Detection Technique 4 — Stress Reverse Engineering

    ⚙️
    Stress Reverse Engineering — Working Backwards From Stated Results
    The ICAAP stress test conclusion is the output. Work backwards to extract the implied assumptions — then test whether they are plausible.

    The Reverse Engineering Method: A window-dressed ICAAP typically cherry-picks optimistic assumptions in each module independently. Individually, each might be defensible. But when you extract all the implied assumptions simultaneously and test whether they are internally consistent and consistent with historical UAE data, the full optimism becomes visible.

    REVERSE ENGINEERING METHODOLOGY — Step by Step Given: Bank ICAAP shows stressed CET1 of 10.8% in Year 3 (passes 7% floor) Starting CET1: 14.1% | RWA: AED 28,000m | Gross loans: AED 20,000m Step 1: Extract implied cost of risk from CET1 decline CET1 fell from 14.1% to 10.8% = −3.3pp over 3 years At RWA 28,000m: total capital erosion = 3.3% × 28,000 = AED 924m over 3 years Add back: retained earnings in stress (PPP − CoR net) must also be estimated If PPP averages AED 168m/yr (PPP/RWA 0.6%) and CoR produces net losses: Annual net loss estimate: solving backwards → CoR implied ~2.2% annualised Step 2: Test implied CoR against stated NPL projection Bank states NPL rises to 8% by Year 3 (from 4.2%) Implied additional Stage 3 loans: 3.8% × 20,000 = AED 760m Implied additional specific provisions: 760 × LGD 30% (stated) = AED 228m/yr Does 228m/yr produce 2.2% CoR? 228 ÷ 20,000 = 1.14% CoR — inconsistently LOW Resolution: Either CoR is understated OR NPL projection is understated 2.2% CoR on 20,000 loans → requires AED 440m provisions At 30% LGD → implies AED 1,467m additional NPLs → NPL ratio 11.6%, NOT 8% CONCLUSION: Bank's NPL stress projection is inconsistently optimistic vs its CET1 decline Step 3: Test implied LGD assumption Bank uses 30% LGD. UAE 2009 actual LGD on RE loans: 40–55%. At 45% LGD: implied additional provisions = 760 × 45% = AED 342m/yr This produces CoR of 1.71% → CET1 drain → Year 3 CET1: 9.8%, not 10.8% Difference: 1.0pp of CET1 = AED 280m of hidden capital gap Step 4: Test simultaneous consistency Apply simultaneously: CoR 2.2% + RWA migration +25% + NIM compression −0.8% Does the ICAAP model apply all three simultaneously? Or sequentially with offsets? If sequential: Year 2 has one shock, Year 3 has another → total stress underestimated If simultaneous: check whether arithmetic holds → usually reveals optimism in at least one FINDING: At least AED 280m additional capital required. P2R add-on warranted.
    Reverse Engineering — Implied vs Stated Assumptions in ICAAP Stress
    Bank states stressed CET1 passes at 10.8%. Reverse engineering shows the internally consistent outcome is 9.1% — a breach.
    7.7 — Evidence Chain

    Building the Evidence Chain — From Clue to Conclusion

    A window dressing finding is not complete until it is expressed as an evidence chain — a logical sequence from observed data point to quantified capital or liquidity impact. Without the chain, the finding is an opinion. With the chain, it is a regulatory case.

    1
    Observation — The Data Anomaly

    State the specific, observable data point that is anomalous. Quantify it. Give the time horizon. Avoid interpretation at this stage.

    Example: "Bank X's Stage 2 ratio rose from 8.0% to 21.0% over 6 quarters (Q1 2023 to Q2 2024) while the NPL ratio remained stable at 4.0–4.2% over the same period. The Stage 2 balance grew from AED 1,600m to AED 4,200m (+AED 2,600m). The NPL balance grew from AED 800m to AED 840m (+AED 40m)."
    2
    Inconsistency Test — What Should Have Happened

    State what relationship between the two data points would be expected in a genuine portfolio, and why the observed relationship violates that expectation.

    Example: "In a genuine portfolio experiencing significant increase in credit risk (as evidenced by Stage 2 growth of AED 2,600m), some proportion of Stage 2 loans would be expected to migrate to Stage 3 (NPL) over the 6-quarter period. The UAE banking industry average Stage 2→3 migration rate for real estate-exposed portfolios is 25–40% per year. At a conservative 25% migration rate, AED 2,600m of Stage 2 growth should have produced at least AED 650m of new Stage 3 loans, raising the NPL ratio by approximately 3.25pp to ~7.45%. The observed NPL stability is arithmetically inconsistent with the observed Stage 2 growth unless: (a) large write-offs removed NPLs from the denominator, (b) large restructurings permanently cured Stage 3 loans back to Stage 2, or (c) Stage 2 classification is being used to defer Stage 3 recognition."
    3
    Alternative Hypothesis Testing — Ruling Out Innocent Explanations

    Test and eliminate each legitimate alternative explanation before concluding window dressing.

    Alternative 1: Large write-offs removed NPLs Write-off rate reported: 2.1% of NPL balance = AED 16.8m/quarter Total write-offs over 6 quarters: ~AED 101m — insufficient to offset AED 650m expected inflows Alternative 2: Genuine Stage 3 cures back to Stage 2 Curing rate from Stage 3 reported: 8% per quarter = AED 67m/quarter Total cures over 6 quarters: ~AED 402m — insufficient to offset inflows Alternative 3: Portfolio genuinely performing better than market Sector context: UAE real estate prices declined 15% over the same 6 quarters Industry NPL in RE sector: rose 3.5pp over same period (peer data) Bank X's RE portfolio NPL: unchanged — INCONSISTENT WITH SECTOR CONCLUSION: Alternatives 1 and 2 together explain only AED 503m of the AED 650m+ gap. Unexplained gap: AED 150m+ of expected NPL inflows not appearing in reported Stage 3. Classification window dressing: probable.
    4
    Quantification — What Is the Capital Impact

    Translate the finding into a specific capital or liquidity impact in AED terms and as a percentage of RWA.

    Shadow NPL estimate: Stage 2 balance: AED 4,200m × 35% historical migration rate = AED 1,470m shadow NPLs Additional specific provision at 45% LGD: AED 1,470m × 45% = AED 662m Less: Stage 2 ECL already raised: AED 4,200m × 3.2% = AED 134m Net additional provision required: AED 528m Post-tax capital impact: AED 528m × (1 − 9%) = AED 481m As % of RWA: AED 481m ÷ AED 28,000m = 1.72% of CET1 Adjusted CET1 ratio: 13.5% − 1.72% = 11.78% True headroom above 7% floor: 4.78% (vs reported 6.5%) True capital runway: (11.78% − 7%) ÷ 1.73% annual drain (Flag 4) = 2.8 years vs reported runway of 4.0+ years
    5
    Regulatory Conclusion — What Action Is Warranted

    State the supervisory action implied by the finding and the additional information required to resolve it.

    Findings summary: 1. Stage 2 accumulation: provisioning shortfall AED 481m (1.72% of RWA) 2. Coverage inflation: narrow coverage 52% vs adequate minimum 70%+ 3. ELAR quarter-end gaming: daily average 9.8% (below 10% minimum) vs reported 16.4% 4. IRB RWA density decline: potential CET1 overstatement of ~0.7% 5. PPP/RWA 0.7%: capital runway 2.8 years under severe stress (below 3-year minimum) Warranted regulatory actions: → P2R add-on: minimum AED 481m (Stage 2 provision gap) + AED 196m (RWA density) = AED 677m additional capital = 2.4% of RWA Adjusted effective CET1 requirement: 7% + 2.4% P2R = 9.4% Against adjusted CET1 of 11.78%: headroom 2.38% — tight → Requirement: raise Stage 2 provision rate to minimum 12% within 2 quarters → Requirement: submit daily ELAR data for 6 months → Management: present credible earnings improvement plan within ICAAP revision

    7.11 — What Good Looks Like

    What a Genuinely Credible ICAAP Looks Like

    Having spent this entire chapter identifying what bad looks like, it is equally important to define the positive standard. A credible ICAAP does not hide vulnerabilities — it acknowledges them, quantifies them, and demonstrates adequate response. The hallmarks:

    ✅
    The Paradox of the Credible ICAAP

    The most credible ICAAP is often the one that shows the most vulnerabilities — because it demonstrates that the bank's management has genuinely looked in the dark corners and reported what they found. An ICAAP that acknowledges a Stage 2 build-up with a plan to address it, shows a capital runway of 3.5 years under severe stress with specific management actions, and honestly states that PPP/RWA needs to improve from 0.7% to 1.5% over 18 months — that ICAAP is more credible than one showing perfect ratios across every dimension. Banks with honest ICAPs typically receive lower P2R add-ons because regulators trust the self-assessment.

    7.12 — The Final Sherlock
    🔍 Be Sherlock Holmes — The Complete Detection Summary
    "You have been in Afghanistan, I perceive." — Everything about the ICAAP tells a story. The question is whether you know how to read it.
    1

    The Seven-Second Screen: Before reading a single page of an ICAAP, run the seven-second quantitative screen: CET1 ratio; PPP/RWA; Stage 2 as % of loans; narrow coverage; ELAR daily average; ASRR; RWA density trend. Any of these outside expected ranges is a flag before you have read a single word of narrative.

    Seven-second screen thresholds: CET1 >14% but PPP/RWA <1%: Flag 4 candidate Stage 2 >15% with NPL <5%: Flag 1 candidate Broad coverage >100% but narrow <60%: coverage inflation candidate ELAR reported vs daily average gap >300bp: period-end gaming candidate RWA density fell >5pp in 4 quarters: model recalibration candidate If 3+ of these trigger: full investigation before reading narrative

    2

    The Narrative Consistency Test: Read the credit risk section of the ICAAP narrative and extract the implied NPL trajectory. Read the capital section and extract the implied CET1 trajectory. Multiply the NPL projection × LGD assumption × provision coverage to get implied impairment charges. Check whether those charges are consistent with the CET1 trajectory. If the capital section and credit section use different implied LGDs or provision rates — even if each section is internally consistent — the overall ICAAP is not.

    Test: Credit section states NPL rises to 9%, LGD 28%, coverage falls to 85%. Capital section shows CET1 falls from 14.1% to 11.2%. Implied additional provisions from credit section: (9%−4.2%)×20,000×28% = AED 269m CET1 decline of 2.9% on 28,000 RWA = AED 812m total erosion Over 3 years at AED 269m/yr provisions = AED 807m — checks out approximately. If they DON'T check out: one section is using different (hidden) assumptions.

    3

    The Footnote Detective: Window dressing is almost never in the headline section — it is in the footnotes, the methodology appendix, and the model assumption tables. The most dangerous footnote is: "Provision rates have been revised to reflect updated economic forecasts." This single sentence can conceal AED 300m+ of provision releases. The footnote detective reads every note and asks: does this change the headline by more than 50bp on any key ratio?

    Footnotes that always warrant investigation: "ECL model recalibrated based on updated macro scenarios" → what changed? by how much? "Collateral valuations updated using management estimates" → independent valuations? "Classification revised following legal review" → are specific loans now Stage 2 instead of 3? "ELAR calculation methodology clarified" → did the eligible asset list change? "RWA calculated using revised probability of default estimates" → by how much? direction? Each of these, individually, is legitimate disclosure. Together, in the same document = pattern.

    4

    The Historical Track Record Test: Request ICAAP documents for three consecutive years. For Year N, extract the stressed NPL, stressed CET1, and stressed cost of risk as projected in the Year N−2 ICAAP. Compare against actual outcomes in Year N. A bank that consistently over-estimates recovery speed and under-estimates stress severity in its ICAAP is not doing a genuine forward-looking assessment — it is producing a document designed to pass rather than to predict.

    Historical accuracy score: For each metric: |actual − projected at N−2| ÷ projected range = accuracy error NPL accuracy error >30%: projection calibration poor CET1 accuracy error >150bp: stress assumptions too mild A bank with systematic optimism bias (actual always worse than projected by similar margin) has a calibration problem that should directly increase P2G.

    5

    The "Where Did the Risk Go?" Final Test: After running all four detection techniques, ask one final question: the bank improved its CET1 by X bp, improved its LCR by Y pp, and maintained a stable NPL ratio. Where did the risk that should have manifested in those ratios actually go? It went somewhere. The job is to find it — in the Stage 2 book, in the ASRR breach, in the PPP/RWA weakness, in the IRB shortfall, or in the ICAAP stress assumptions themselves. The risk conservation principle guarantees it is there. The detective's job is to find it before it finds the bank.

    Final test framework: Total risk improvement claimed (in ratio terms): ΣΔ(positive changes) Total risk deterioration found (in other ratios): ΣΔ(negative changes) Conservation check: are these approximately equal? If claimed improvements >> observed deteriorations: unobserved risk likely remains Location candidates: Stage 2 book, ASRR advances, IRB provision shortfall, ICAAP scenario optimism, off-balance sheet exposures The gap between what improved and what deteriorated = the hidden risk quantum
