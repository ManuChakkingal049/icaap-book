3.1 — The Story

The Desert Traveller and the Water Canteen

A Beginner's Parable

A traveller sets off across the UAE desert. A wise sheikh offers two pieces of advice. First: "You must carry enough water to survive for at least two days if you find no oasis." That is your LCR — a minimum 30-day liquid buffer for a crisis. Second: "Your water supply must come from reliable deep wells, not seasonal streams that dry up in summer." That is your NSFR — stable funding matched to your journey length.

Now a different traveller — a local Bedouin who knows every oasis, every reliable water source, and every seasonal stream — does not need the same rules. His Bedouin council has its own standard: "You must always carry water from the three permanent deep wells near Al Ain" (ELAR — only the most reliable sources count). And: "You cannot use more water than your reliable wells can produce each day" (ASRR — advances must not exceed stable resources).

The global rules assume you are crossing an unknown desert. The UAE rules recognise who is making the journey and what water sources actually exist here. Same destination — stay alive. Different maps. Both valid.

The rulebook changes based on who is making the journey — but the destination is identical: survive a severe liquidity stress without central bank support.
3.2 — Why They Exist

Why Liquidity Ratios Exist

🟢 Simple Version — The Bank Run

A bank can be perfectly solvent — owning valuable assets worth more than its debts — and still fail in 48 hours if enough depositors all try to withdraw at once. The assets (long-term loans, property) cannot be turned into cash quickly enough to pay everyone. Liquidity ratios measure whether the bank holds enough instantly-cashable assets to survive a mass withdrawal, and whether its funding structure is stable enough that the mass withdrawal is less likely to happen in the first place.

Technical explanation: Banks are structurally illiquid by design — they borrow short (deposits, overnight repo) and lend long (mortgages, project finance). This maturity transformation is how banks add economic value, but it creates inherent vulnerability. A sudden loss of confidence — even in a solvent bank — can trigger a funding run that turns a liquidity problem into a solvency problem as asset fire sales at distressed prices crystallise losses.

Northern Rock (2007) was solvent when depositors queued outside its branches. It failed because its wholesale funding model could not survive market closure. Basel III's LCR and NSFR were designed specifically to prevent this. The UAE's ELAR and ASRR were designed to prevent the same outcome in the specific context of UAE banking structure.

💡
The Two Dimensions of Liquidity Risk

Short-term (30-day) survival: Can the bank survive a sudden market stress — wholesale market closure, mass retail deposit run — using only its pre-positioned liquid asset buffer? This is what LCR and ELAR measure. Structural funding stability (1-year): Is the bank's funding structure sound enough that a short-term disruption cannot become a long-term crisis? This is what NSFR and ASRR measure. Both dimensions are required in ICAAP/ILAAP.

3.3 — LCR

LCR — Liquidity Coverage Ratio

LCR Formula — Basel III
LCR = High Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) ÷ Net Cash Outflows (30 days) × 100 Minimum requirement: 100% (fully phased since 2019) Net cash outflows = Stressed gross outflows − Stressed inflows (capped at 75% of outflows)
Source: BCBS, "Basel III: The Liquidity Coverage Ratio and liquidity risk monitoring tools," January 2013. BIS.

Why This Bank Fails LCR

The GRE and government deposit base (AED 2,000m at 40% outflow = AED 800m stressed outflow) is the critical driver. This is the UAE structural problem — if these deposits were classified as stable (as their long-term nature might suggest), the outflow would be only AED 100m and LCR would pass at 127%. But Basel treats them as non-operational wholesale. This is one of the primary reasons the CBUAE designed ELAR differently — it does not stress-test outflows at all, using total liabilities instead.

3.5 — The Paradox

The LCR/NSFR Paradox — How a Bank Can Pass LCR but Fail NSFR

This is one of the most analytically important insights in liquidity analysis, and one that trips up even experienced practitioners. A bank can have a strong LCR while simultaneously failing NSFR — and the reason reveals a fundamental design difference between the two ratios.

The Mechanism: Buying HQLA With Short-Term Wholesale Funding

ScenarioHQLAShort-Term WholesaleLong-Term LoansLCRNSFRWhat Is Hidden
Starting position40060050076% fail76% failBoth fail — mismatch visible
Option A — Buy HQLA with wholesale600 (+200)800 (+200)500180% pass ↑91% fail ↑LCR fixed cosmetically; NSFR partially improved but structural mismatch remains
Option B — Issue long-term debt600 (+200)600 (unchanged)500175% pass ↑138% pass ↑Genuine fix — both ratios improve

Why Option A Improves LCR But Not NSFR

The Arithmetic of the Paradox (Option A)
Borrow AED 200 additional short-term wholesale → Buy AED 200 Level 1 HQLA LCR impact: Numerator +200 (HQLA added at full value) Denominator +80 (200 wholesale × 40% outflow rate — only partial) → LCR rises from 158% to 180% ← looks better! NSFR impact: ASF: new wholesale 200 × 0% ASF factor = +0 (zero stable funding added) RSF: new HQLA 200 × 0% RSF factor = −85 (old "other assets" 85% RSF replaced) → NSFR rises from 76% to 91% ← improved but still failing The structural problem: AED 500 long-term loans still funded by volatile short-term money. LCR asks: "Can we survive 30 days?" → Yes, we have more HQLA now. NSFR asks: "Is our year-long funding structure sound?" → No, it is worse (more wholesale).
Key insight: The 40% LCR outflow rate on wholesale is less than 100%, so adding HQLA funded by wholesale always mechanically improves LCR regardless of whether it addresses the structural mismatch.
⚠️
What Happens on Day 31?

After Option A, the bank's LCR looks excellent at 180%. But on day 31, the bank must roll AED 800m of short-term wholesale debt. If the market is closed — as wholesale markets were for weaker UAE names in 2009 — the bank has no mechanism to refinance. The HQLA buffer was consumed meeting day 1–30 outflows. The long-term loan book cannot be sold. The bank fails at exactly the moment LCR predicted it would survive. NSFR was designed to catch this cliff edge.

3.6 — UAE Framework Overview

UAE Framework — Why the CBUAE Created Different Ratios

The CBUAE operates a two-tier liquidity framework. Category 1 banks (large, internationally active) must comply with Basel LCR and NSFR. Category 2 banks (smaller, domestic-focused) use ELAR and ASRR instead. Category 1 banks must also report ELAR as a supplementary metric.

Bank CategoryPrimary Liquidity RatiosSupplementaryTypical Profile
Category 1LCR (≥100%) + NSFR (≥100%)ELAR (≥10%) as supplementaryFAB, ENBD, ADCB, DIB, Mashreq, large foreign branches
Category 2ELAR (≥10%) + ASRR (≤100%)Voluntary NSFR encouraged by CBUAESmaller domestic banks, Islamic banks, specialist lenders
3.7 — The 5 Reasons (Small Bank View)

The 5 Reasons — Why LCR/NSFR Are Not Suitable for Small UAE Banks

This is the most important section of this chapter for UAE practitioners. Each of the five reasons is explained first in simple terms, then technically, then from the perspective of a small UAE Category 2 bank — showing exactly what LCR/NSFR would require and why ELAR/ASRR solves the problem differently.

1
Reason 1 — Data & Operational Complexity

Simple: LCR requires a small bank to build expensive computer systems to track every deposit by type, counterparty, and maturity daily. ELAR requires knowing your total liabilities — a number already on every bank's balance sheet.

Technical: LCR's denominator (stressed net cash outflows) requires: daily 30-day cash flow projection; behavioural outflow rate per product type; counterparty classification per deposit; off-balance sheet drawdown assumptions; intraday liquidity tracking; and a 75% inflow cap calculation. This demands a dedicated treasury data warehouse, sophisticated product tagging, and daily treasury operations. For a small UAE bank with AED 8bn in assets and a 20-person treasury team, this infrastructure costs AED 30–50m to build and AED 8–12m per year to maintain.

Small UAE bank reality: A Category 2 bank with 15 branches, 45,000 retail customers, and 800 SME clients must — under full LCR — classify each SME deposit as operational vs non-operational, determine counterparty type, track contractual vs behavioural maturity, and recalculate daily. ELAR replaces all of this with: Eligible Liquid Assets ÷ Total Liabilities. One number. Already in the general ledger.

LCR problem: AED 40m+ infrastructure investment required ELAR solution: Uses existing GL data — zero new infrastructure
2
Reason 2 — GRE and Government Deposit Concentration

Simple: UAE small banks are often funded significantly by government-related deposits — municipality accounts, utility company accounts, local authority treasuries. Basel treats these as risky (40% outflow rate). But in practice, these deposits are often semi-permanent — they stay for years. ASRR addresses this by simply giving government deposits low stability weighting rather than assigning a daily cash flow stress.

Technical: Under Basel LCR, non-operational government and GRE deposits receive a 40% outflow rate. For a small UAE bank with 35% of its funding from government-related entities, this creates an enormous 30-day stressed outflow that requires a proportionally enormous HQLA buffer. ASRR instead assigns a 0–20% stability weight to GRE deposits in the stable resources denominator — it acknowledges they are not fully reliable without forcing the bank to hold 40% of them in liquid assets.

Small UAE bank reality — numerical illustration:

Small Category 2 bank balance sheet: Total assets: AED 8,000m GRE/govt deposits: AED 2,800m (35% of funding) Retail deposits: AED 2,200m Interbank: AED 1,000m LCR treatment of GRE deposits: 2,800 × 40% outflow = AED 1,120m stressed outflow in 30 days HQLA required just for GRE outflow: AED 1,120m As % of total assets: 14% — very high for small bank Cost of holding this liquidity: ~AED 28m p.a. (yield differential on HQLA vs loans) ELAR treatment: GRE deposits counted at face value in total liabilities denominator No special outflow rate — just contributes to the 10% ELAR requirement HQLA needed for GRE portion: 2,800 × 10% = AED 280m (vs AED 1,120m under LCR) Cost saving vs LCR: AED 840m less HQLA needed = ~AED 21m p.a. in opportunity cost
LCR problem: 40% outflow rate makes GRE-funded banks hold 4× more HQLA ELAR solution: Treats GRE deposits proportionally via 10% overall buffer
3
Reason 3 — Islamic Banking and Sharia-Compliant HQLA

Simple: Islamic banks cannot hold conventional interest-bearing bonds. But Basel's HQLA consists mostly of interest-bearing government bonds. For an Islamic bank, the HQLA universe under Basel is tiny. ELAR's eligible assets include CBUAE-issued wakalah certificates — Sharia-compliant equivalents — making the ratio achievable.

Technical: Level 1 HQLA under Basel includes conventional sovereign bonds, agency bonds, and central bank deposits. Islamic banks cannot hold conventional coupon-bearing bonds — they earn and pay interest (riba), which is prohibited. The UAE sovereign sukuk market, while growing, is insufficient in supply for all Islamic banks to hold enough Level 1 HQLA simultaneously. Forcing Islamic banks to comply with full Basel LCR would either force Sharia violations or make LCR compliance mathematically impossible given the supply constraint.

The wakalah solution:

Standard Basel Level 1 HQLA: conventional treasury bonds, CDs, reserves Islamic bank constraint: CANNOT hold conventional coupon bonds (riba prohibition) CBUAE wakalah certificate (Islamic CD): Structure: Bank places funds with CBUAE as wakeel (agent) CBUAE invests in Sharia-compliant asset pool Bank receives profit rate (not interest) — fully Sharia-compliant Maturity: Short-term (overnight to 3 months) — highly liquid Issuer: CBUAE — sovereign equivalent credit risk Supply: CBUAE controls issuance to ensure sufficient supply for all Islamic banks Under ELAR: wakalah certificates count as eligible liquid assets at full value Under LCR: would count as Level 1 equivalent — BUT supply has historically been insufficient UAE Islamic bank market share: ~25% of total banking assets If all Islamic banks needed to hold Basel LCR HQLA, UAE sukuk supply would need to be ~3× current outstanding issuance — practically impossible without CBUAE-issued instruments
LCR problem: Insufficient Sharia-compliant HQLA supply in UAE market ELAR solution: Wakalah CDs explicitly included; supply guaranteed by CBUAE
4
Reason 4 — USD Peg and Limited Lender of Last Resort

Simple: The CBUAE cannot print unlimited AED to rescue banks the way the Bank of England can print sterling — because every AED must be backed by USD reserves to maintain the peg. This makes self-sufficiency in liquidity more important, and ELAR's mandatory pre-positioned buffer more critical than Basel's assumption of central bank backstop.

Technical: Under the impossible trinity, the UAE chose a fixed exchange rate (peg to USD at 3.6725) and free capital flows, sacrificing independent monetary policy. A central bank with independent monetary policy can create unlimited domestic currency in a crisis — the Fed can print dollars to backstop US banks. The CBUAE can provide AED liquidity but each AED created must be backed by USD reserves. In a severe banking crisis requiring emergency liquidity of AED 20bn, the CBUAE would need to deploy USD 5.4bn of reserves — a significant constraint on the lender of last resort function.

Small UAE bank implication:

In a free-floating currency country (e.g. UK): Bank runs short of GBP liquidity → Bank of England creates GBP → provides ELA (Emergency Liquidity Assistance) → crisis contained Cost to central bank: essentially zero (can create its own currency) Pre-positioned bank buffer: important but central bank is a reliable backstop In UAE (pegged currency): Bank runs short of AED liquidity → CBUAE must deploy USD reserves to create AED USD reserves as of 2024: ~USD 185bn (CBUAE + ADIA combined) If 3 medium-sized banks need emergency AED simultaneously: AED 15bn each = AED 45bn total = USD 12.2bn of reserves deployed This is ~6.6% of total reserves — significant constraint ELAR implication: Small UAE banks MUST hold sufficient HQLA independently. The CBUAE backstop is available but not unlimited. ELAR ensures each bank maintains a self-sufficient 30-day buffer regardless of central bank support. ELAR minimum of 10% is calibrated to this constraint — not to Basel's assumption of unlimited domestic currency creation.
LCR problem: Assumes unlimited central bank backstop — not true for pegged currency ELAR solution: Mandates self-sufficient pre-positioned buffer; calibrated to peg constraint
5
Reason 5 — Thin/Developing Capital Markets

Simple: Basel HQLA assumes you can sell assets quickly in a crisis without moving the price. In UAE's developing capital markets, there may simply not be enough buyers when all banks sell at once — making an asset technically classified as HQLA practically illiquid in a stress.

Technical: Basel's HQLA classification assumes active secondary markets with sufficient depth that large sales do not significantly move prices. For Level 2A and 2B assets specifically, Basel assumes price declines of no more than 15–40% in a 30-day stress. These assumptions were calibrated to US treasuries, German bunds, and covered bond markets — markets with daily volumes of hundreds of billions. UAE capital markets, while growing, have daily secondary trading volumes orders of magnitude smaller.

Market depth comparison — numerical:

Asset class liquidity comparison (approximate 2023 data): US treasury market: Daily secondary volume ~USD 650bn → Deep German bunds: Daily secondary volume ~USD 25bn → Deep UK gilts: Daily secondary volume ~USD 18bn → Deep UAE sovereign bonds/sukuk: Daily secondary volume ~USD 40m → Very thin UAE corporate bonds: Daily secondary volume ~USD 8m → Almost illiquid UAE covered bonds: DO NOT EXIST in UAE market UAE RMBS: Embryonic — occasional issuance only If UAE bank needs to sell AED 500m of UAE sovereign bonds in 30-day stress: That represents ~13× daily UAE secondary market volume Price impact: potentially 15–25% (far exceeding Basel's 0% Level 1 assumption) Effective recovery: AED 375–425m, not AED 500m ELAR solution: Restricts eligible assets to CBUAE-redeemable instruments only. Bank does NOT sell in the secondary market — it redeems directly with CBUAE. No market depth required. No price impact. Liquidity is guaranteed by the central bank, not by an assumption about market depth that does not exist in the UAE.
LCR problem: L1/L2 eligibility assumes market depth that UAE does not have ELAR solution: Eligible assets are central-bank-redeemable — no market depth needed

Why ELAR's Asset Universe Is Narrower Than Basel HQLA

ELAR deliberately restricts eligible assets to instruments where liquidity is guaranteed by the CBUAE itself — not by secondary market depth. A bank holding ELAR-eligible assets does not need to find a buyer in a stress — it redeems directly with the CBUAE. This is a fundamentally more reliable form of liquidity in a market where secondary trading volumes are thin. ELAR trades breadth of eligible assets for certainty of liquidity — which is exactly the right trade-off for the UAE's developing capital market context.

3.9 — ASRR Deep Dive

ASRR — Advances to Stable Resources Ratio

ASRR Formula — CBUAE
ASRR = Total Advances ÷ Stable Resources × 100 Maximum: 100% — LOWER is safer (opposite direction to NSFR!) Advances = all loans and customer financing (Islamic or conventional) Stable Resources = equity + long-term debt + weighted deposits
Source: CBUAE Liquidity Regulations for Banks in the UAE, 2015 (updated 2023).

ASRR vs NSFR — They Are Structural Inverses

The Inverse Relationship
NSFR = Stable Funding I HAVE ÷ Stable Funding I NEED → want > 1 (HIGHER safer) ASRR = What I Have LENT ÷ Stable Funding I HAVE → want < 1 (LOWER safer) If you strip away weighting differences, ASRR ≈ 1 ÷ NSFR (approximately) NSFR 125% ≈ ASRR 80% — same underlying message: stable funding exceeds lending NSFR 80% ≈ ASRR 125% — same problem: lending exceeds stable funding
They are NOT perfect reciprocals because: (1) NSFR covers the entire balance sheet while ASRR covers only advances; (2) RSF factors in NSFR are more granular than ASRR stability weights; (3) NSFR captures investment securities mismatch that ASRR misses entirely.

⚠️
The ASRR Blind Spot — Investment Securities

ASRR only captures advances (loans) in the numerator. A small UAE bank could borrow AED 500m short-term wholesale and invest it in a sovereign bond portfolio — completely invisible to ASRR. ASRR would show the bank passing (loans are funded by stable resources) while NSFR would flag the mismatch (securities funded by short-term wholesale). This is why CBUAE encourages larger Category 2 banks to voluntarily report NSFR — to catch exactly this gap. See Chapter 7 for the detailed flag analysis.

ASRR Failing — Why and What the Bank Must Do

The bank fails ASRR at 122.5% because AED 2,800m of GRE deposits — 35% of total funding — contributes only AED 280m (10%) to stable resources, yet the entire lending book of AED 6,100m must be funded by those resources. The GRE deposits are funding AED 1,120m of loans (the 22.5% excess) with no stable resource backing. Options: reduce the loan book by AED 1,120m; raise AED 1,120m of retail deposits or long-term debt; or reduce GRE deposit reliance and replace with stable retail funding. The CBUAE will scrutinise this bank closely — ASRR breaches in the UAE context signal exactly the 2009-type structural vulnerability.

Why LCR Would Be Even Harder — The GRE Outflow Effect

Under LCR, AED 2,800m of GRE deposits generate AED 1,120m of stressed outflows — more than the entire HQLA buffer of AED 1,150m. To pass LCR at 100%, this bank would need to more than double its HQLA to AED 1,890m — requiring an additional AED 740m of liquid assets that would otherwise be deployed as loans. This is operationally and commercially unsustainable for a small bank, which is precisely why CBUAE created ELAR.

3.12 — Window Dressing

How Window Dressers Hide — Liquidity Ratios

🎭
How Window Dressers Hide in Liquidity Ratios
Four techniques for making ELAR, ASRR, LCR and NSFR look better than true liquidity position — and the footprints they leave
1
Period-End Repo Dressing — Making ELAR and LCR Flash Green at Quarter-End

How They Hide: In the days before quarter-end reporting, the bank borrows short-term in the repo market (using its existing bond portfolio as collateral) and deposits the cash proceeds at the CBUAE. This temporarily increases eligible liquid assets, boosting ELAR. The repo is unwound the day after reporting. The bank's daily ELAR for the quarter may average 7.8% — well below the 10% minimum — but reports 11.2% at quarter-end.

Bank's true daily ELAR average (Jan–Mar): 7.8% (below 10% minimum) Quarter-end window dressing: Mar 28: Repo AED 280m (30-day overnight repo, collateral = UAE bonds held) Mar 28: Cash proceeds deposited at CBUAE → eligible liquid assets +280m March 31 reported ELAR: (1,150 + 280) ÷ 7,000 = 20.4% ← looks excellent! April 2: Repo matures, proceeds returned, ELAR falls back to 11.6% (bonds now "pledged") True daily ELAR (non-window-dressed): 7.8% Regulatory impact: Bank reports passing ELAR — but has been non-compliant all quarter

Footprint & Detection: Regulators with daily LCR/ELAR reporting requirements (increasingly standard) will see the spike on quarter-end dates vs daily averages. Compare the 90-day average ELAR to the quarter-end ELAR. A pattern of quarter-end spikes followed by immediate decline is a clear signal. Also check: does the repo book spike at quarter-end? Does the CBUAE reserve balance jump at quarter-end?

2
ASRR Numerator Suppression — Reclassifying Loans to Reduce Advances

How They Hide: Moving loans out of the "advances" classification and into "investment securities" or "treasury assets" categories to reduce the ASRR numerator. Commodity murabaha transactions, reverse repos structured as financing, and certain trade finance products sit in a grey zone between "advances" and "investments." Banks may classify them as investments — correctly in legal form — to exclude them from the ASRR numerator, even though economically they represent customer credit exposure.

Bank ASRR failing at 122.5%: Advances: 6,100m | Stable resources: 4,980m → 122.5% (breach) Window dressing: reclassify AED 1,200m commodity murabaha as "treasury investments" New reported advances: 4,900m Stable resources: 4,980m (unchanged) New reported ASRR: 4,900 ÷ 4,980 = 98.4% ← just passing! Economic reality unchanged: AED 1,200m is customer financing — credit risk exposure to corporate counterparties Economic ASRR: 6,100 ÷ 4,980 = 122.5% ← still breaching Regulatory test: Is this financing extended to a customer for their operational/investment need? If yes → it is an advance regardless of legal form → must be in numerator

Footprint & Detection: Compare ASRR advances to total credit RWA from the capital calculation. Credit RWA captures all customer financing regardless of accounting classification. If ASRR advances are materially lower than credit RWA-implied loan volume, reclassification may have occurred. Request a reconciliation between ASRR advances and credit RWA exposures.

3
GRE Deposit Misclassification — Calling Unstable Deposits Stable

How They Hide: GRE deposits in ASRR receive 0–20% stability weight. But if the bank reclassifies a large GRE deposit as a "retail deposit" (because the GRE entity has a retail account number) or a "long-term deposit" (because it has a nominal 7-month term, despite having a break clause), it receives 100% stability weight instead. A AED 500m GRE deposit reclassified from 10% to 100% stability boosts stable resources by AED 450m — worth ~9 percentage points of ASRR improvement.

Current ASRR: 6,100 ÷ 4,980 = 122.5% (breach) GRE deposit: AED 500m held by Abu Dhabi municipality treasury Correct classification: GRE deposit → 10% stability = AED 50m stable resources Window dressing: classify as "long-term deposit" due to 7-month nominal term (despite break clause allowing withdrawal in 48 hours) New stability weight: 100% = AED 500m stable resources Stable resources with reclassification: 4,980 + 450 = 5,430m New ASRR: 6,100 ÷ 5,430 = 112.3% ← still breaching but looks better If 3 such deposits reclassified: stable resources = 6,330m → ASRR = 96.4% ← passing! Reality: These deposits can leave in 48 hours. The "stable resource" is illusory.

Footprint & Detection: Review the counterparty classification of the top-20 deposits. Any deposit from a government ministry, municipality, sovereign wealth fund, or state-owned enterprise should be classified as GRE regardless of account type. Request a breakdown of deposits classified as "long-term" by counterparty type — GRE deposits with nominal long-term structures but break clauses should be reclassified at the effective demand rate.

4
ELAR Asset Eligibility Overstatement — Including Ineligible Assets

How They Hide: Including assets in ELAR eligible liquid assets that do not meet the CBUAE's strict eligibility criteria — typically GRE bonds with non-zero risk weights, or foreign sovereign bonds from issuers not on the CBUAE's approved list. A GRE sukuk that carries a 20% risk weight (not zero) is not ELAR-eligible — but its exclusion may not be noticed in a cursory regulatory review.

Bank's actual ELAR position: Cash + CBUAE instruments: 800m → eligible ✓ UAE federal sovereign bonds: 350m → eligible ✓ TOTAL ELIGIBLE: 1,150m ELAR = 1,150 ÷ 7,000 = 16.4% ← passing Incorrect inclusion (window dressing): Add: Abu Dhabi GRE sukuk (20% risk weight): 400m → NOT eligible (not 0% RW) Add: Bahrain sovereign bonds (not on CBUAE approved list): 150m → NOT eligible Window-dressed ELAR: (1,150 + 550) ÷ 7,000 = 24.3% ← inflated Impact of correction: True ELAR stays at 16.4% — still passing in this case But if true HQLA were only 680m: window-dressed ELAR = (680+550)÷7,000 = 17.6% (pass) Correct ELAR = 680÷7,000 = 9.7% (FAIL)

Footprint & Detection: Request a security-level breakdown of every asset claimed as ELAR-eligible. For each: verify the issuer, verify the risk weight assignment (should be 0% for sovereign/federal, check for GRE bonds specifically), verify the issuer is on the CBUAE's published approved list. GRE bonds with 20% risk weights are a common incorrect inclusion.

3.13 — Detection
🔍 Be Sherlock Holmes — Detecting Liquidity Window Dressing
"The world is full of obvious things which nobody by any chance ever observes." — The liquidity clues are always visible to those who know where to look.
1

The Quarter-End Spike Clue: Compare the 90-day moving average of ELAR/LCR to the quarter-end reported figure. A bank with genuine liquidity maintains consistent ratios throughout the quarter. A window dresser's ratio spikes on reporting dates and falls immediately after.

Test: Request daily ELAR for each month of the quarter. Red flag: Quarter-end ELAR > 90-day average by more than 300bp Also check: Repo book size at quarter-end vs mid-quarter CBUAE reserve balance at quarter-end vs daily average Formula: Window dressing indicator = (Quarter-end ELAR − Average ELAR) ÷ Average ELAR If this ratio > 20%, investigate quarter-end repo activity.

2

The Advances Reconciliation Clue: Compare total ASRR advances to total credit risk RWA. Credit RWA is calculated on all customer financing — it cannot be reduced by reclassification the way ASRR advances can. If ASRR advances are significantly lower than what credit RWA implies, financing has been reclassified out of the numerator.

Test: Implied advances from credit RWA = Credit RWA ÷ average risk weight (typically 60–80%) Example: Credit RWA = AED 5,800m, avg risk weight = 75% → Implied advances = AED 7,733m If ASRR reports advances of AED 5,400m → gap = AED 2,333m likely reclassified Threshold: ASRR advances should be within 15% of credit-RWA-implied advances Larger gap: investigate treasury/investment vs advances classification boundary

3

The GRE Classification Clue: For any deposit above AED 100m in the "retail" or "long-term" stable category, request the counterparty name. Government ministries, municipality treasuries, sovereign wealth funds, and state-owned enterprises should never be classified as retail deposits or treated as 100% stable regardless of account structure.

Test: Request top-50 deposits by counterparty name and classification For each deposit in stable/retail category > AED 50m: - Is the counterparty a government entity? → reclassify as GRE (0–20% stable) - Does the deposit have a break clause? → treat as demand deposit for ASRR - Has the counterparty historically broken deposits early? → apply lower stability Red flag: Any single deposit > AED 200m classified as "stable retail"

4

The ELAR Asset Audit Clue: Request a security-by-security listing of every asset in the ELAR eligible pool. For each, verify: (1) the issuer is on the CBUAE's published eligible list; (2) the risk weight is 0% (for UAE sovereign/federal) or as specified; (3) the asset is unencumbered (not pledged as collateral elsewhere).

Common incorrect inclusions in UAE banks' ELAR: — GRE sukuk with 20% risk weight (should be excluded) — Bahrain/Oman sovereign bonds not on CBUAE approved list — Encumbered bonds (pledged as repo collateral — not freely available) — Bonds with maturity <30 days that have already matured but not been removed Test: ELAR eligible assets should be ≤ (cash + CBUAE instruments + UAE federal bonds + approved foreign sovereign) Any ELAR figure significantly higher than this benchmark warrants detailed audit

5

The Cross-Ratio Consistency Clue: ELAR, ASRR, LCR and NSFR should tell a consistent story. A bank passing ELAR comfortably while failing ASRR badly has a structural mismatch — it has enough liquid assets for a 30-day snapshot but has funded its loans with unstable money. A bank passing LCR while failing NSFR is using HQLA purchased with wholesale funding to game the short-term metric. When ratios diverge, the more structural ratio (NSFR, ASRR) is usually telling the truth.

Consistency test matrix: If ELAR passing AND ASRR failing: GRE deposit structural mismatch — serious If LCR passing AND NSFR failing: HQLA gaming with wholesale — investigate If ASRR passing AND NSFR failing: Bond portfolio mismatch — ASRR blind spot If all passing: Verify each ratio independently for window dressing The most dangerous position: ELAR comfortable (16%+) but ASRR failing (120%+) This means the bank has enough cash for 30 days but its loan book has no stable funding.
