5.1 — The Story

The Rainfall and the Reservoir

A Beginner's Parable

Think of a bank's CET1 capital as a large desert reservoir. It was built up over years of careful engineering (retained earnings from past profitable years). Now, in a drought (economic stress), the reservoir is the only water source. Holes are appearing in the walls (credit losses eroding capital). The question ICAAP asks is not just "how full is the reservoir today?" but "how much rain is falling to refill it?"

A bank with CET1 of 14% but PPP/RWA of 0.4% has a large reservoir in a drought with almost no rainfall. Every hole in the wall empties it permanently. A bank with CET1 of 12% and PPP/RWA of 3.2% has a smaller reservoir but sits under steady rain. Even as holes appear, the water level barely drops — the rainfall fills them faster than the holes drain.

Here is where it gets funny: the banker with the big empty reservoir keeps telling the board "we have excellent capital ratios!" The banker with the steady rainfall says "our CET1 looks modest today, but watch what happens in Year 3 of your stress scenario." Year 3 arrives. The first banker is calling investors for an emergency capital raise. The second banker is issuing dividends. Capital today is history. Earnings are the future. ICAAP cares about both.

Capital today is a snapshot of history. Pre-provision profit is the engine that determines where capital goes next.
5.2 — Why They Matter in ICAAP

Why Profitability Ratios Matter in ICAAP

🟢 Simple: The Three Roles of Profitability in ICAAP

(1) Capital generation: Profits retained after dividends are the primary engine of CET1 growth. A bank that earns more than it loses in credit costs continuously builds capital without needing to raise new equity. (2) Loss absorption buffer: Pre-provision profit must first absorb credit losses before they hit CET1. The bigger the earnings buffer, the more losses a bank can absorb without eroding capital. (3) Capital raisability: A bank with strong, consistent earnings trades at or above book value — making new equity issuance feasible at acceptable dilution. A bank with deteriorating earnings trades at a discount — new capital is expensive or impossible, precisely when it is most needed.

Technical explanation: In ICAAP stress testing, the income statement is projected forward over a 3–5 year horizon. The projection works as follows:

The ICAAP P&L to CET1 Chain
Net Interest Income (NIM × earning assets) + Non-Interest Income (fees, commissions, FX, insurance) = Total Operating Income − Operating Expenses (staff, premises, IT, depreciation) = Pre-Provision Profit (PPP) ← the earnings buffer − Impairment Charge (cost of risk × loan book) = Profit Before Tax − Tax (UAE: 9% CIT on qualifying income since June 2023) = Net Profit After Tax − Dividends paid = Retained Earnings added to CET1
In a stress scenario: if Impairment Charge > PPP, the bank makes a net loss. Every AED of net loss reduces retained earnings and therefore CET1 directly. PPP/RWA determines how large the impairment charge must be before this happens.
5.3 — Return on Equity
ROE — Return on Equity
ROE = Net Profit After Tax ÷ Average Shareholders' Equity × 100
🟢 Simple: The Shareholder's Report Card

ROE tells shareholders: for every AED 100 they invested, how many AED did the bank earn this year? A 12% ROE means shareholders earned AED 12 on each AED 100 invested. If the cost of that equity (what shareholders expect to earn given the risk) is 10%, the bank created AED 2 of value per AED 100. If ROE is below the cost of equity, the bank is destroying value — it would be better for shareholders to invest elsewhere.

The DuPont Decomposition — the most important ROE insight:

DuPont Decomposition
ROE = ROA × Equity Multiplier (Assets ÷ Equity) = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover × Equity Multiplier A stable ROE can mean: (A) ROA rising + leverage stable → genuine improvement ✓ (B) ROA falling + leverage rising → leverage compensation ✗ (window dressing signal) (C) ROA stable + leverage stable → steady state, sustainable ✓

5.4 — Return on Assets
ROA — Return on Assets
ROA = Net Profit After Tax ÷ Average Total Assets × 100
🟢 Simple: Stripping Out the Leverage Effect

ROE can be boosted by borrowing more (leverage). ROA strips that out — it tells you how much profit the bank earns purely from deploying its asset base, regardless of how those assets are funded. A high ROE driven by a shrinking equity base (rising leverage) is dangerous. A high ROA driven by genuine efficiency is healthy. In ICAAP, ROA is the cleaner metric of underlying business quality.

ROA and ROE are mathematically linked: ROE = ROA × (Total Assets ÷ Equity). This relationship is the key to spotting the leverage compensation window dressing pattern.

The Leverage Compensation Treadmill

ROE is stable at 12.0% throughout. But the equity multiplier has risen from 10× to 13.3× — meaning the leverage ratio has fallen from 10.0% to 7.5%. CET1 ratio is drifting down even though reported profit is stable. This is the "treadmill" — the bank must grow assets faster than NIM is falling just to maintain the same absolute profit. When the treadmill eventually stops (asset growth slows or NIM hits a floor), profit collapses and there is less capital to absorb it.

5.5 — Net Interest Margin & IRRBB
NIM — Net Interest Margin & IRRBB
NIM = Net Interest Income ÷ Average Interest-Earning Assets × 100
🟢 Simple: The Spread Between Borrowing and Lending

NIM is the fundamental business of banking in one number. If a bank borrows deposits at 2% and lends mortgages at 5.5%, its gross NIM is 3.5%. After funding costs, overheads, and provisions, that 3.5% spread generates the profit. A falling NIM means the bank is either paying more for its funding, earning less on its loans, or both. In UAE banking, NIM is heavily influenced by the EIBOR rate (Emirates Interbank Offered Rate) — the local equivalent of SOFR/LIBOR.

NIM and IRRBB — The Pillar 2 Connection

NIM is where IRRBB (Interest Rate Risk in the Banking Book) becomes a Pillar 2 capital risk. Banks typically have asset-liability mismatches in interest rate tenor — they may lend at fixed rates and fund at floating, or vice versa. When rates change, NIM changes — but by different amounts on the asset side and liability side depending on the mismatch.

5.6 — Cost-to-Income Ratio
CIR — Cost-to-Income Ratio
CIR = Operating Expenses ÷ Operating Income × 100 (lower is better)
🟢 Simple: How Much of Every AED of Income Is Eaten by Costs

CIR tells you what percentage of income disappears before a single AED of credit loss is absorbed. A CIR of 60% means AED 40 of every AED 100 of income reaches pre-provision profit. A CIR of 38% means AED 62 reaches pre-provision profit. In a stress scenario where income falls 25%, the high-CIR bank's PPP collapses by 62% (from 40 to 15 per 100 of original income) while the low-CIR bank's PPP falls only 40% (from 62 to 37). High CIR is the amplifier that turns an income shock into a capital crisis.

This is the "double whammy" of a high-CIR bank in a stress: income falls (NIM compression, lower volumes) while costs remain largely fixed, wiping out PPP before credit losses even begin.

5.7 — PPP/RWA
PPP/RWA — The Most Important ICAAP Profitability Metric
PPP/RWA = Pre-Provision Profit ÷ Risk-Weighted Assets × 100
🟢 Simple: The Earnings Firewall

PPP/RWA measures how much profit the bank generates per unit of risk before any credit losses. It is the firewall between credit losses and CET1. A bank with PPP/RWA of 3.0% can absorb a stressed cost of risk of 2.5% and still be profitable — the 0.5% surplus adds to CET1. A bank with PPP/RWA of 0.8% is wiped out by any cost of risk above 0.8% — every AED beyond that hits CET1 directly.

The PPP/RWA Absorption Test
If PPP/RWA > stressed Cost of Risk: Bank is profitable → CET1 grows → passes stress If PPP/RWA < stressed Cost of Risk: Bank makes loss → CET1 erodes → may breach floor Annual CET1 drain under stress = (Cost of Risk − PPP/RWA) × (1 − tax rate) Example: Cost of Risk 2.5%, PPP/RWA 0.6%, Tax 9% Annual drain = (2.5% − 0.6%) × (1 − 9%) = 1.73% of RWA per year Capital runway = (CET1 − 7% floor) ÷ 1.73% = 4.0 years maximum
This formula gives the "capital runway" — how many years of stress the bank can survive before breaching the regulatory floor. Regulators expect a minimum runway of 3–5 years under severe stress.

5.8 — Three Levers to Improve PPP/RWA

Three Levers to Genuinely Improve PPP/RWA

When a bank's ICAAP reveals weak PPP/RWA as a primary risk, the management actions section must present credible steps to improve it. There are only three genuine levers — everything else is either cosmetic or temporary.

1
Grow the Numerator — Improve Pre-Provision Profit
Raise NIM: Reprice existing loans upward, shift mix toward higher-yielding products, reduce funding costs by growing retail deposits and reducing expensive wholesale.

Grow fee income: Transaction banking, trade finance, wealth management, bancassurance — all generate non-interest income with zero or minimal RWA consumption.

Cut costs: Reduce CIR through digital transformation, branch rationalisation, procurement efficiency. Takes 12–18 months to deliver.
50bp NIM rise on AED 18bn earning assets = AED 90m more PPP = +0.32% PPP/RWA AED 40m fee income addition = +0.14% PPP/RWA (zero RWA cost) CIR from 55% to 45% on AED 1,120m income = AED 112m more PPP = +0.40% PPP/RWA
2
Shrink the Denominator — Reduce RWA Efficiently
Exit low-yielding, high-risk-weight assets. Replace 3% margin loans at 100% risk weight with 4.5% margin loans at 75% risk weight — both numerator improves AND denominator falls simultaneously.

This is genuine RWA optimisation (improving the risk-return profile) vs cosmetic RWA optimisation (IRB model recalibration without changing the portfolio).
Replace AED 1,000m corporate loans (3% margin, 100% RW) with AED 1,000m retail mortgages (3.8% margin, 65% RW): PPP numerator: +AED 8m (higher margin) RWA denominator: −350m (35% lower risk weight) Net PPP/RWA improvement: +0.09% direct + denominator effect
3
Restructure the Business Model
For structurally weak PPP/RWA, the real answer is often fundamental change: exit unprofitable segments, consolidate branches, digitise to reduce cost base structurally, or merge for scale.

UAE Example — FAB creation: The 2017 merger of NBAD and First Gulf Bank created First Abu Dhabi Bank. The combined entity achieved PPP/RWA improvement through scale economies, reduced duplication, and improved pricing power. Both constituent banks had individually compressed NIM and high relative CIR.
NBAD pre-merger PPP/RWA (est): ~1.8% FGB pre-merger PPP/RWA (est): ~2.2% FAB post-merger PPP/RWA: ~2.8% (scale + cost synergies + pricing power) CIR improvement: ~8-10pp reduction over 3 years post-merger

The Core ICAAP Insight — Capital Today vs Capital Tomorrow

The starting capital ratio tells you where you are. The PPP/RWA tells you where you will be.

5.10 — UAE Context (Standalone)
🇦🇪 UAE Specific — Standalone Section

Profitability in UAE Banking — Structural Characteristics

UAE Corporate Income Tax — Impact on Profitability Ratios (Since June 2023)

The UAE introduced a 9% Corporate Income Tax (CIT) on taxable profits above AED 375,000 effective for financial years starting 1 June 2023. This materially impacts profitability ratios for UAE banks for the first time. Pre-CIT, UAE banks reported near-full pre-tax profits as post-tax profits. Post-CIT, net profit is reduced by ~9% of taxable income, and therefore retained earnings added to CET1 are reduced proportionately.[5]

NIM in the UAE Rate Environment

UAE bank NIM is heavily correlated with EIBOR (Emirates Interbank Offered Rate), which tracks the US Federal Funds Rate given the AED/USD peg. The 2022–2023 Fed rate hiking cycle (0% to 5.25%) materially boosted UAE bank NIM — particularly for banks with floating-rate corporate loan books that repriced faster than their retail deposit costs. UAE D-SIBs saw NIM expand from ~2.6% in 2021 to ~3.8–4.2% in 2023–2024. Any future rate cutting cycle by the Fed would compress UAE NIM — ICAAP must stress test this sensitivity.

Islamic Banking Profitability — Different but Comparable

Islamic banks in the UAE report equivalent profitability metrics using Islamic finance terminology: Return on investment (ROI) equivalent to ROA; financing margin equivalent to NIM (the profit rate on Islamic financing products minus the return paid to investment account holders). Islamic banks typically achieve slightly higher financing margins because murabaha and ijara structures often carry slightly higher effective margins than equivalent conventional loans, partly due to Sharia compliance costs being priced in.

🇦🇪
IRRBB in UAE Banks — A Growing ICAAP Concern

The 2022–2024 rate cycle exposed a significant IRRBB vulnerability in UAE banks with large fixed-rate mortgage portfolios. While rising rates boosted NIM on corporate books, banks with substantial AED fixed-rate residential mortgages (originated at 3–4% in 2018–2020) saw these assets become loss-making relative to new funding costs at 5%+. The CBUAE has intensified its focus on IRRBB quantification in ICAAP submissions following this experience. Banks must now model a +200bp and −200bp parallel shift, a steepening, and a flattening scenario for the banking book — all four scenarios producing a capital add-on estimate.

5.12 — Window Dressing

How Window Dressers Hide — Profitability Ratios

🎭
How Window Dressers Hide in Profitability Ratios
Four techniques that inflate reported earnings without improving the recurring income engine — and the footprints they always leave

Profitability window dressing is particularly dangerous because it directly inflates the capital base (via retained earnings) while leaving the underlying earnings weakness intact. A CET1 ratio inflated by non-recurring income looks identical to one inflated by genuine earnings — until the next stress removes the non-recurring income and the ratio collapses.

1
One-Time Disposal Gains — Non-Recurring Profit Boosts CET1 Permanently

How They Hide: Selling investment securities, properties, or subsidiary stakes at a gain books a one-time profit flowing to retained earnings and CET1. Reported ROE and ROA look dramatically improved. But the asset generating the gain is gone — future income is lower, and the bank's recurring earnings quality is unchanged or worse.

UAE Numerical Example:

Bank's recurring profile: NIM: 2.1% | CIR: 63% | PPP/RWA: 0.62% | Net profit: AED 240m | ROE: 6.8% (Below cost of equity — bank under earnings pressure) Window dressing — sell UAE sovereign bond portfolio: Bonds purchased 2018 at par (AED 800m face value) Current market value: AED 952m (rates fallen since purchase) Gain on disposal: AED 152m (pre-tax) Post-tax gain at 9% CIT: AED 138m Reported results with disposal: Net profit: AED 240m + AED 138m = AED 378m Reported ROE: 378 ÷ 3,500 = 10.8% ← looks acceptable Reported PPP/RWA (inc. disposal): (0.62% effective) ← unchanged! CET1 boost from disposal: +138 ÷ 28,000 = +0.49% Reality: Recurring net profit: AED 240m → core ROE: 6.8% — below cost of equity Future NIM reduced: bond portfolio replacing loan income now gone Replacement investment at current rates = lower yield = future NIM compression Core PPP/RWA: 0.62% → ICAAP stress shows CET1 breach in Year 3

Footprint & Detection: Decompose the income statement into recurring (NII + fee income − opex) and non-recurring (disposal gains, insurance settlements, revaluation credits). Calculate "core ROE" and "core PPP/RWA" on recurring income only. If reported ROE is 10.8% but core ROE is 6.8% for two consecutive years, the bank's recurring earnings are deteriorating behind a facade of asset sales. Also check: what replaced the sold asset on the balance sheet? Reinvested at a lower yield? Then future NIM is lower — the gain today is a loan on future income.

2
Leverage Compensation — Stable ROE Hiding Falling ROA and Thinning Capital

How They Hide: As NIM falls, the bank grows assets and leverage to maintain absolute profit and therefore ROE. The ratio looks stable quarter-on-quarter. But ROA is falling, the equity multiplier is rising, and the leverage ratio (and CET1 ratio) is deteriorating silently every quarter.

Four-Quarter Treadmill Example:

Q1: NIM 3.8% | ROA 1.2% | Equity multiplier 10.0× | ROE 12.0% | Leverage ratio 10.0% Q2: NIM 3.5% | ROA 1.1% | Equity multiplier 10.9× | ROE 12.0% | Leverage ratio 9.2% Q3: NIM 3.2% | ROA 1.0% | Equity multiplier 12.0× | ROE 12.0% | Leverage ratio 8.3% Q4: NIM 2.9% | ROA 0.9% | Equity multiplier 13.3× | ROE 12.0% | Leverage ratio 7.5% ROE is perfectly flat at 12.0% — board sees "stable, consistent performance" Leverage ratio: fallen from 10.0% to 7.5% — 250bp silent deterioration in 4 quarters CET1 ratio: thinning as RWA grows faster than retained earnings can build capital Tipping point: If NIM falls to 2.6% and bank cannot grow assets further: Net profit collapses from AED 480m to AED 355m ROE falls from 12.0% to 8.7% — first visible signal But CET1 ratio is already 250bp lower than it was at Q1 There is far less capital to absorb the coming income shock

Footprint & Detection: Always decompose ROE into ROA × equity multiplier. Plot both on separate trend lines over 8 quarters. Falling ROA + rising multiplier + stable ROE = compensation treadmill. Calculate "normalised CET1 trajectory" — what happens to CET1 if the bank stops growing assets and ROE falls to ROA × current equity base? Also monitor leverage ratio trend — a falling leverage ratio is the direct measurement of this problem.

3
Provision Release to Inflate Quarterly Earnings

How They Hide: Releasing previously raised provisions back through the P&L in periods of earnings pressure — citing improved economic outlook or revised ECL model assumptions — to turn a quarterly net loss into a profit. This inflates reported PPP, ROA, ROE, and the period's retained earnings. But it is non-recurring: the provisions must eventually be re-raised when conditions deteriorate, creating a future P&L charge.

Quarterly Smoothing Example:

Q3 — NIM compressed, fee income flat, bank under pressure: Pre-provision profit: AED 185m (genuine) New provisions required: AED 240m Expected net loss (before release): AED 55m × (1−9%) = AED 50m after tax Window dressing — management revises Stage 2 ECL assumptions: Stage 2 book: AED 3,200m Previous Stage 2 provision rate: 8.0% → provisions AED 256m New Stage 2 provision rate: 5.5% (citing "improved GCC economic outlook") Provision release: 3,200 × 2.5% = AED 80m released to income Reported Q3: Net impairment: AED 240m − AED 80m release = AED 160m Reported profit: AED 185m − AED 160m = AED 25m × (1−9%) = AED 23m Reported quarterly ROA: +0.20% | Reported PPP/RWA: appears strong Q4 reality — Stage 2 re-provisions required (conditions did not improve): Must raise AED 80m of provisions that were released in Q3 PLUS new provisions Q4 impairment charge: AED 240m + AED 80m catch-up = AED 320m Q4 net loss: AED 320m − AED 185m PPP = AED 135m loss Annual smoothing effect: zero — total provisions for the year unchanged Damage: board was misled in Q3; risk management credibility damaged

Footprint & Detection: Track net impairment charge (gross provisions minus releases) by quarter. A "lumpy" pattern — large Q4 charges and Q1 releases — while underlying credit quality appears stable is a provision management signal. Compare Stage 2 ECL provision rate quarter-over-quarter — a sharp downward revision in a stable or deteriorating credit environment is a red flag. Calculate the 8-quarter rolling average cost of risk and compare each quarter to this average; deviations of more than ±0.5% without an identifiable credit quality driver warrant investigation.

4
Fee Income Front-Loading — Booking Multi-Year Arrangement Fees Upfront

How They Hide: Booking multi-year loan arrangement fees, insurance commissions, or trade finance fees in the origination period rather than amortising them over the life of the transaction. This inflates non-interest income in origination-heavy quarters and deflates it in subsequent periods. PPP/RWA, ROA, and ROE look strong in origination periods — masking the underlying recurring income weakness.

Numerical Example:

Project finance loan originated: AED 1,200m, 7-year term, 2.0% arrangement fee Correct IFRS 9 treatment: amortise fee over 7 years as part of effective interest rate Annual fee income recognition: AED 1,200m × 2.0% ÷ 7 years = AED 3.4m per year Window dressing — book fee upfront: Year 1 fee income: AED 1,200m × 2.0% = AED 24m (full upfront) Years 2–7: fee income AED 0 (already booked) Year 1 impact on PPP/RWA: Additional non-interest income: AED 24m − AED 3.4m = AED 20.6m inflated PPP/RWA inflation: AED 20.6m ÷ AED 28,000m RWA = +0.07% per transaction If bank originates 15 such transactions per year: Total annual fee inflation: AED 20.6m × 15 = AED 309m PPP/RWA inflation: +1.1% — material! Cliff effect: in Year 2, PPP/RWA collapses by 1.1% as front-loaded fees are not replaced

Footprint & Detection: Request a breakdown of non-interest income by recognition method (upfront vs amortised). Compare fee income growth against loan origination volumes — if fees grow 30% while originations grew 8%, front-loading is likely. Monitor the deferred fee income liability on the balance sheet — a declining deferred fee balance while originations are flat signals prior front-loading being unwound. Also check: does the bank's fee income show a cyclical pattern correlated with origination volumes rather than the underlying loan book size?

5.13 — Detection
🔍 Be Sherlock Holmes — Detecting Profitability Window Dressing
"Elementary, my dear Watson — but only if you know where to look." Five analytical tests that expose what the headline ROE is hiding.
1

The Core Earnings Separation Clue: Always decompose reported net profit into (a) recurring net interest income + fee income − opex, (b) one-time disposal gains and revaluation credits, and (c) provision releases. Calculate core PPP/RWA using only (a). If reported PPP/RWA is 2.8% but core PPP/RWA is 1.1%, the bank's recurring earnings are structurally weak regardless of the headline.

Test formula: Core PPP/RWA = (NII + recurring fees − opex) ÷ RWA Red flag: (Reported PPP/RWA − Core PPP/RWA) > 0.5% for 2+ consecutive years This gap represents non-recurring income the ICAAP stress cannot rely upon. Also flag: if "other income" grows faster than total assets by more than 10% per year, investigate each component for one-time items.

2

The ROE Decomposition Clue: Always split ROE = ROA × equity multiplier and plot both on the same chart over 8 quarters. If ROA is falling while the multiplier is rising and ROE is stable, the bank is running the leverage compensation treadmill. Calculate the leverage ratio (equity ÷ total assets) — a falling leverage ratio alongside stable ROE is the primary signal.

Test: ROE decomposition quarterly over 8 periods If ROA falls >0.1% per year AND equity multiplier rises >0.5× per year → flag Leverage ratio test: if leverage ratio falls >50bp per year with stable ROE → compensation Normalised ROE = ROA × historical average equity multiplier (5-year avg) If normalised ROE is materially below reported ROE → leverage is doing the work

3

The PPP Runway Clue: Calculate the capital runway explicitly: (Current CET1 − 7%) ÷ Annual CET1 drain in stress = years to floor. If this number is less than 3 years under the bank's own "severe stress scenario," the bank has a profitability problem regardless of what the current CET1 ratio shows.

Test: Capital runway = (CET1 − 7%) ÷ [(stressed CoR − PPP/RWA) × (1 − tax)] Example: CET1 14%, PPP/RWA 0.6%, stressed CoR 2.5%, tax 9%: Annual drain = (2.5% − 0.6%) × 91% = 1.73% Runway = (14% − 7%) ÷ 1.73% = 4.0 years Minimum acceptable under severe stress: 5+ years If runway < 3 years: ICAAP must include specific earnings improvement plan If runway < 2 years: P2G likely; P2R add-on possible

4

The NIM Sustainability Clue: Compare current NIM to the bank's 5-year historical average. If current NIM is above the 5-year average, test whether it is sustainable or driven by a temporary rate environment. For UAE banks: calculate NIM sensitivity to a −200bp rate scenario (Fed cutting cycle). If NIM falls by >100bp under this scenario, the current NIM is not sustainable and the ICAAP stress test must reflect the lower level.

Test: NIM stress scenario Current NIM: 3.8% (elevated by 2022-2023 rate cycle) 5-year historical average NIM: 2.9% Fed cutting cycle scenario (−200bp EIBOR by 2026): NIM impact −80bp → NIM 3.0% PPP/RWA impact: −80bp × earning asset ratio ÷ RWA = approximately −0.7% PPP/RWA If ICAAP does not model this NIM compression: PPP/RWA in stress is overstated by 0.7% Correct stressed PPP/RWA: 3.8% − 0.7% (NIM) − 0.3% (volume) = 2.8% (not the 3.8% reported)

5

The Fee Income Quality Clue: Request a breakdown of non-interest income into: transactional (recurring, volume-based), structural (relationship-based advisory), and episodic (one-time arrangement fees, disposal gains). The ICAAP stress scenario should apply a 30–40% haircut to transactional income (volumes fall in stress) and a 70–90% haircut to episodic income (non-recurring). If the ICAAP applies the same moderate haircut to all three categories, it is overstating stressed earnings.

Test: Non-interest income stress decomposition Transactional fees (card, FX, remittance): apply 35% stress haircut Structural fees (advisory, syndication): apply 50% stress haircut Episodic income (disposals, arrangement upfront): apply 90% stress haircut Example: Reported non-interest income AED 420m Transactional: AED 200m × 65% = AED 130m stressed Structural: AED 120m × 50% = AED 60m stressed Episodic: AED 100m × 10% = AED 10m stressed Total stressed non-interest income: AED 200m (vs reported AED 420m, −52%) If ICAAP uses only −20% haircut: stressed PPP/RWA overstated by ~0.8%
