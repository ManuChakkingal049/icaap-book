    The Leaking Bucket and the Slow Drain

    A Beginner's Parable

    Imagine a bank's loan book as a large bucket of water. Every time a borrower repays their loan, fresh clean water flows in. Every time a borrower defaults, the bucket develops a small hole and water drips out. The NPL ratio tells you what percentage of your bucket is already dripping. The coverage ratio tells you how many buckets of sawdust (provisions) you have ready to absorb the drips before they hit the floor. The cost of risk tells you how fast new holes are appearing.

    Now here is the tricky part that trips up most analysts: you can have a bucket with only three small holes (low NPL ratio, excellent coverage) but twenty hairline cracks forming around the rim that nobody has noticed yet (large Stage 2 book). The bucket looks fine from a distance. But one good knock — a recession, a GRE default, a property correction — and all twenty cracks split open simultaneously. The water floods out faster than any provision bucket can absorb it.

    The art of credit quality analysis is finding the hairline cracks before the knock arrives. This chapter teaches you where to look.

    The NPL ratio shows you the holes already open. The Stage 2 ratio shows you the cracks forming. A good analyst reads both.
    4.2 — Why They Exist

    Why Credit Quality Ratios Exist

    🟢 Simple Version — The Milk Test

    When you check if milk is still good, you do not wait until it smells terrible (Stage 3 default). You check the colour (NPL ratio — is it visibly off?), you check the smell regularly (cost of risk — is it getting worse each week?), and you check whether there is enough fresh milk in reserve to replace it if it goes bad (coverage ratio — do we have enough provisions?). Credit quality ratios are the milk tests for a bank's loan book.

    Technical explanation: Banks extend credit knowing some loans will default. The key regulatory and management questions are: (1) how much of the existing book has already deteriorated? (2) is the rate of new deterioration accelerating? (3) are adequate provisions held against expected losses? and (4) in a stress scenario, does the bank hold enough capital to absorb losses beyond its provision buffer?

    Under IFRS 9 (effective January 2018), banks moved from an "incurred loss" model (recognise loss only when it has happened) to an Expected Credit Loss (ECL) model — requiring forward-looking provisions as soon as credit risk increases, even before a default event. This makes credit quality ratios more forward-looking but also creates more room for management judgement and — as we will see — for window dressing.

    IFRS 9 ECL — The Three-Stage Framework

    Capital: Specific provision hits CET1
    Non-Performing Loan (NPL)
    💡
    The Unlikely To Pay (UTP) Criterion — Often Missed

    Under Basel and IFRS 9, a loan becomes Stage 3 / NPL not only when it is 90+ days past due but also when the bank concludes the borrower is unlikely to pay in full regardless of payment status. A borrower making minimum payments through a restructured agreement but fundamentally insolvent should be classified Stage 3 (UTP). Many UAE banks apply the 90-day rule but use discretion on UTP — choosing a narrow interpretation to keep loans in Stage 2. This is one of the primary Stage 2 window dressing mechanisms.

    4.4 — NPL Ratio

    NPL Ratio — The Stock of Bad Debt

    NPL Ratio Formula
    NPL Ratio = Non-Performing Loans (Stage 3) ÷ Gross Loans × 100 NPL = loans where: (a) 90+ days past due on any material obligation, OR (b) Bank concludes borrower unlikely to pay in full (UTP) Gross loans = total loan book BEFORE deducting provisions
    Sources: Basel II Framework, para 452–457 (default definition). CBUAE Circular on Loan Classification and Provisioning. IFRS 9 Stage 3 = credit-impaired (IAS 39 definition of impaired).
    🟢 Simple: The Three Uses of the NPL Ratio

    The NPL ratio answers three questions at once: (1) How bad is it today? — the absolute level tells you where you stand. (2) Is it getting better or worse? — the trend over 8 quarters tells you the direction. (3) How do we compare? — against peers and UAE benchmarks tells you whether this is a bank problem or an industry problem. A rising NPL in isolation could be aggressive recognition (honest). A rising NPL across all UAE banks signals a systemic credit cycle.

    The Bathtub Model — Stock vs Flow

    The NPL ratio is a stock measure — it tells you the level at a point in time. But a stable NPL ratio can conceal two very different realities: a bank genuinely recovering (new NPL inflows slowing, old NPLs resolving) vs a bank masking deterioration (new NPL inflows rising but being offset by forced write-offs). The bathtub metaphor is essential:

    The NPL Bathtub Model
    Closing NPL = Opening NPL + New NPL inflows (Stage 2 → Stage 3 migration) - Recoveries (Stage 3 → Stage 2 curing) - Write-offs (NPLs removed from the book after full provisioning) A stable NPL ratio can mean: (A) Inflows slow + few write-offs = genuine improvement (B) Inflows fast + many write-offs = bank clearing the deck aggressively (C) Inflows fast + zero write-offs = bank avoiding recognition, NPL building
    Case (C) is the window dressing scenario. A bank with high new NPL inflows but zero write-offs is accumulating a hidden non-performing portfolio.
    4.5 — Coverage Ratio

    Coverage Ratio — Narrow vs Broad

    Two Versions of Coverage Ratio
    Narrow Coverage = Specific Provisions (Stage 3) ÷ NPLs (Stage 3) × 100 Broad Coverage = (Specific + General Provisions) ÷ NPLs × 100 Narrow = honest; compares like-for-like (Stage 3 provisions vs Stage 3 loans) Broad = common in reporting; inflated by Stage 1/2 provisions against PERFORMING loans
    Note: Broad coverage can exceed 100% even when specific coverage is only 60–70% — because general provisions on the performing book are added to a smaller NPL denominator. This is the primary coverage window dressing mechanism.
    🟢 Simple: Why Broad Coverage Is Misleading

    Imagine you have AED 100 of unpaid invoices (NPLs). You have AED 70 set aside specifically for those invoices (specific provisions = 70% narrow coverage). You also have AED 50 set aside for future invoices that might go bad (general provisions). Broad coverage says (70+50) ÷ 100 = 120% — excellent! But that AED 50 is reserved for future bad debts in your performing book, not for the AED 100 already overdue. If you use that AED 50 now, you have no cushion for the next wave. Broad coverage mixes two different provisions serving two different purposes and presents them as one comforting number.

    ⚠️
    The Hidden Risk in This Bank

    The 110% broad coverage looks reassuring. But if even 30% of the Stage 2 book (AED 960m) migrates to Stage 3: new NPLs = 800 + 960 = AED 1,760m. General provisions (AED 280m) will be consumed providing Stage 3-specific provisions. New broad coverage = (600 + specific increase) ÷ 1,760. The bank needs an additional AED 768m of provisions in a single stress event. That is an AED 768m P&L charge directly reducing CET1. The comfortable broad coverage ratio concealed this entirely.

    4.6 — Cost of Risk

    Cost of Risk — The Flow of New Losses

    Cost of Risk Formula
    Cost of Risk = Impairment Charge for Period ÷ Average Gross Loans × 100 (annualised) Also expressed as: Net credit loss rate | Credit cost ratio | Loan loss rate Relationship to P&L: Cost of Risk × Loan Book = Annual Impairment Expense → This flows through to: Profit after tax → Retained earnings → CET1
    The most direct link between credit quality and capital adequacy in ICAAP stress testing.
    🟢 Simple: The Bathtub Flow Rate

    If the NPL ratio is the water level in the bathtub, cost of risk is the speed at which water is flowing in. A stable NPL ratio with rising cost of risk means the tap is opening faster but someone is also pulling the plug (write-offs). Eventually the tap outpaces the drain. Cost of risk rising from 0.8% to 2.5% on a AED 20bn loan book means an extra AED 340m of annual P&L charge flowing straight to CET1 erosion.

    Cost of Risk vs Pre-Provision Profit — The Absorption Test
    When cost of risk exceeds PPP/RWA, every AED of impairment hits CET1 directly. This is the PPP/RWA flag from Chapter 7.
    4.7 — Loan-to-Deposit Ratio

    Loan-to-Deposit Ratio (LDR)

    LDR Formula
    LDR = Gross Loans ÷ Customer Deposits × 100 Complementary metric — sits at the intersection of credit quality and liquidity. Measures: how much of the loan book is funded by deposits (the most stable funding).
    Not a formal Basel ratio — but monitored by CBUAE as a key structural indicator. Related to ASRR (Chapter 3) but simpler. Does not adjust for deposit stability — raw comparison only.
    LDR LevelInterpretationUAE ContextICAAP Implication
    Below 80%Conservative — excess deposits fund liquid assetsUAE Islamic banks (asset-light) often hereExcess deposits → HQLA → helps ELAR; may indicate under-deployment
    80–95%Normal operating range — deposits broadly fund loansUAE large conventional bank averageICAAP: monitor wholesale funding gap carefully
    95–110%Elevated — wholesale/interbank funding needed for the gapSome UAE SME-focused banks post-2020ICAAP: ASRR likely stressed; wholesale dependency rising
    Above 110%Stressed — significant non-deposit funding dependencyPre-crisis UAE banks 2007–2008ICAAP: concentration risk add-on; funding plan required
    🇦🇪
    LDR and ASRR — Reading Them Together in UAE Context

    LDR and ASRR measure similar structural risks but with different lenses. LDR treats all deposits equally regardless of stability. ASRR assigns stability weights — meaning a UAE bank with 90% LDR but 50% GRE deposits will look fine on LDR but potentially breach ASRR (because GRE deposits contribute near-zero to stable resources). In UAE ICAAP analysis, always read LDR and ASRR together. An LDR of 90% with ASRR of 115% signals exactly the GRE structural mismatch that caused problems in 2009.

    4.8 — LTV Ratio

    LTV Ratio — The Collateral Cushion

    LTV Formula
    LTV = Outstanding Loan Balance ÷ Current Market Value of Collateral × 100 Lower LTV = more collateral cushion between loan balance and collateral value. LTV > 100% = loan exceeds collateral value → bank cannot recover in full if borrower defaults.
    Collateral values must be CURRENT — not original at origination. Using stale valuations is the primary LTV window dressing technique. CBUAE regulations require annual independent valuations on commercial real estate above AED 5m.

    LTV Stress Test — UAE Property Price Shock

    The UAE experienced a ~50–60% property price decline in 2008–2009 in some segments. ICAAP stress tests for UAE RE-heavy banks must model severe price declines and recalculate LTV distribution across the mortgage and commercial real estate book.

    The −45% Shock: 50% of the Book Is Under-Collateralised

    Under the −45% property stress (within range of UAE 2009 experience), approximately 50% of this mortgage portfolio becomes under-collateralised. Banks cannot fully recover outstanding balances through collateral enforcement. The LGD on these loans rises from an assumed 20–25% to potentially 40–60%. This translates directly into additional ICAAP provision requirements and CET1 erosion. The LTV distribution — not just the average LTV — is what matters. A portfolio average LTV of 65% can contain a dangerous tail of high-LTV loans.

    4.9 — Connection to ICAAP

    How Credit Quality Ratios Connect to the ICAAP Picture

    🟢 Simple: The Domino Chain

    Property prices fall (LTV deteriorates) → borrowers default (NPL ratio rises) → more provisions needed (cost of risk spikes) → provisions exceed pre-provision profit (earnings wiped out) → net losses erode retained earnings (CET1 falls) → bank may breach the 7% regulatory floor (ICAAP breach). Every credit quality ratio is a domino in this chain. Monitoring all of them together tells you how close the chain is to tipping.

    4.10 — UAE Context (Standalone)
    🇦🇪 UAE Specific — Standalone Section

    Credit Quality in UAE Banking — Structural Characteristics

    UAE banks face credit quality dynamics that differ materially from European or Asian peers, driven by the structure of the UAE economy, the GRE-dominated corporate sector, and the property-centric wealth model.

    UAE-Specific Credit Risk Factor 1 — Real Estate Cycle Dominance

    Dubai and Abu Dhabi experienced sharp property price declines of 50–60% in 2008–2009 and 20–30% in 2014–2016 (oil price correction). Banks with high real estate and construction loan concentrations saw NPL ratios peak at 10–15%+ during these cycles. The CBUAE mandates specific stress testing of property price declines in all UAE banks' ICAAP submissions. The 2008–2009 combination of property collapse + GRE defaults + wholesale funding closure is considered the baseline "severe stress scenario."

    UAE-Specific Credit Risk Factor 2 — GRE Single-Name Concentration

    Several large UAE banks have individual GRE exposures representing 8–15% of their loan book. When a GRE undergoes restructuring — as Dubai World, Nakheel, and Limitless did in 2009–2011 — the concentration creates correlated losses that a diversified portfolio would not experience. The CBUAE's single obligor limit of 25% of capital base provides some protection, but below this level, dangerous concentration can exist without triggering regulatory limits.

    CBUAE Minimum Provision vs IFRS 9 ECL — The Higher of Principle

    The CBUAE requires banks to maintain provisions at the higher of (a) the CBUAE minimum provision percentages above and (b) the IFRS 9 ECL calculation. In practice, IFRS 9 ECL often produces higher provisions for Stage 2 and Stage 3 loans in a stressed environment. UAE banks must reconcile both frameworks and use the more conservative outcome. The CBUAE 1% general provision on the performing book is also a hard floor — below which general provisions cannot fall regardless of IFRS 9 ECL outcomes.

    4.12 — Window Dressing

    How Window Dressers Hide — Credit Quality Ratios

    🎭
    How Window Dressers Hide in Credit Quality Ratios
    Four techniques — each exploiting the gap between accounting rules and economic reality in loan classification and provisioning

    Credit quality window dressing is the most consequential form because it directly conceals future capital losses. All four techniques exploit the same fundamental gap: IFRS 9 requires significant management judgement in classifying loans and measuring ECL. That judgement creates flexibility — and flexibility creates opportunity to present a rosier picture.

    1
    Stage 2 Accumulation — Suppressing NPL Ratio by Keeping Defaulters in Stage 2

    How They Hide: Banks keep deteriorating loans in Stage 2 by (a) evergreening — extending new credit so borrowers make overdue payments, resetting the 90-day clock; (b) restructuring — modifying terms to prevent the 90-day trigger being reached; (c) weak Unlikely-To-Pay application — requiring explicit evidence of imminent collapse before classifying UTP, even when fundamental insolvency is clear. Meanwhile, large general provision pools are accumulated to maintain attractive broad coverage ratios.

    Six-Quarter Divergence — UAE Property Developer Example:

    Bank has AED 2,000m exposure to UAE real estate developers. Real estate market declined 25% over 6 quarters. Three developers behind on payments. Q1: NPL 4.0% | Stage 2: 8% | Broad coverage: 110% — clean Q2: NPL 4.0% | Stage 2: 11% (developer A restructured — not NPL) | Coverage: 112% Q3: NPL 4.1% | Stage 2: 14% (developer B evergreened — clock reset) | Coverage: 115% Q4: NPL 4.1% | Stage 2: 17% (developer C "special mention") | Coverage: 118% Q5: NPL 4.2% | Stage 2: 19% | Coverage: 120% — market sees: excellent coverage! Q6: NPL 4.2% | Stage 2: 21% | Coverage: 122% Q7 — Property falls further 10%, developers cannot service restructured terms: Developer A: NPL (UTP confirmed) → AED 600m Stage 3 Developer B: NPL (evergreen credit exhausted) → AED 450m Stage 3 Developer C: NPL (payment finally missed 90 days) → AED 380m Stage 3 New NPLs in Q7: AED 1,430m NPL ratio jumps: 4.2% → 4.2% + (1,430÷20,000) = 11.4% in ONE QUARTER Broad coverage collapses: was 122%, now 68% Additional impairment charge required: ~AED 900m → CET1 falls ~3.2% of RWA

    Footprint & Detection: Plot NPL ratio AND Stage 2 ratio on the same chart over 8 quarters. A flat NPL ratio with a rising Stage 2 ratio is the primary signal. Also track: forbearance ratio (restructured/modified loans as % of total); new NPL inflow rate vs write-off rate (if inflows are high but NPL stable, someone is evergreening or writing off aggressively); and Stage 2 provision rate vs historical default LGD (if Stage 2 provision rate is 4% but migrated Stage 2 loans historically have 25% LGD, provisioning is insufficient).

    2
    Coverage Inflation — Using General Provisions on Performing Loans to Disguise Weak Specific Provisioning

    How They Hide: Raise large Stage 1 and Stage 2 general provisions to inflate the broad coverage ratio numerator, while keeping specific provisions on Stage 3 NPLs artificially low (by assuming optimistic recovery rates on collateral). The broad coverage ratio soars to 120%+ while narrow coverage is only 55–60% — well below what actual LGD experience would support.

    Numerical Example:

    Bank has: Gross loans AED 20,000m | Stage 3 NPLs AED 800m Genuine provisioning (based on actual LGD experience: 45%): Specific provisions required: 800 × 45% = AED 360m Narrow coverage: 360 ÷ 800 = 45% — clearly inadequate Window dressing: Specific provisions reported: AED 360m (only what's hard to argue against) General provisions (Stage 1/2): AED 520m (elevated ECL assumptions on performing book) Broad coverage reported: (360 + 520) ÷ 800 = 110% ← looks excellent! Narrow coverage reported: 360 ÷ 800 = 45% ← buried in a footnote Reality test: If 30% of Stage 2 (AED 960m) migrates to Stage 3 and LGD is 45%: Additional specific provision needed: 960 × 45% = AED 432m General provisions consumed from Stage 2 pool: ~AED 128m (Stage 2 provisions released) Net additional P&L charge: AED 432m − 128m = AED 304m CET1 impact: AED 304m × (1−9% tax) = AED 277m = ~1.0% of CET1 ratio

    Footprint & Detection: Always calculate both narrow and broad coverage independently. If the gap exceeds 30 percentage points, investigate the general provision assumptions. Compare the Stage 2 ECL provision rate against the bank's own historical data on Stage 2 → Stage 3 migration and post-migration LGD. A Stage 2 provision rate of 4% when historical LGD on migrated Stage 2 loans is 30% represents a 26% under-provisioning gap on the Stage 2 book.

    3
    Stale Collateral Valuations — Hiding LTV Deterioration to Suppress Provision Requirements

    How They Hide: Using outdated or peak-market collateral valuations to understate current LTV, which reduces the provision requirement on secured loans. After a 25% property price decline, a bank that continues to use 2022 peak valuations on its real estate collateral will show LTVs of 65% when true current LTVs are 87% — just over the threshold where full recovery becomes questionable.

    Numerical Example:

    Loan: AED 4,500m real estate portfolio (mix of residential and commercial) Original 2022 valuations: AED 7,200m → LTV = 4,500÷7,200 = 62.5% (comfortable) Dubai property index decline 2023–2024: approximately 20% from 2022 peak Current correct valuations: 7,200 × (1−20%) = AED 5,760m Current true LTV: 4,500 ÷ 5,760 = 78.1% — elevated but below 100% Bank continues using 2022 valuations: Reported LTV: 62.5% — no additional provision required True LTV: 78.1% — requires LGD uplift in IFRS 9 ECL model IFRS 9 ECL difference: At 62.5% LTV, LGD assumption: 15% → Stage 2 ECL per AED 1,000m at-risk: AED 150m At 78.1% LTV, LGD assumption: 32% → Stage 2 ECL per AED 1,000m at-risk: AED 320m Hidden provision requirement: AED 170m per AED 1,000m of at-risk Stage 2 RE loans Total hidden provision gap (Stage 2 RE = AED 2,000m): AED 340m

    Footprint & Detection: Cross-reference reported portfolio LTV against published property price indices. For UAE residential: use REIDIN, ValuStrat, or CBRE Dubai/Abu Dhabi indices. For commercial: JLL UAE commercial property reports. If the bank's reported average LTV has not moved in 4 quarters while the relevant property index has fallen 15–20%, valuations are stale. Require the date of last independent valuation for all commercial real estate above AED 5m (CBUAE mandates annual independent valuation above this threshold).

    4
    Cost of Risk Smoothing — Provision Release to Inflate Current Period Earnings

    How They Hide: Releasing previously raised provisions back through the P&L — either citing improved credit conditions or arguing that provisions were conservatively over-estimated — to boost reported net profit in a period where the bank is under earnings pressure. A negative impairment charge (provision release) turns a quarterly net loss into a profit without any genuine improvement in the underlying credit book.

    Numerical Example:

    Q3 position: Bank under earnings pressure (NIM compressed, fee income flat) Recurring pre-provision profit: AED 185m Gross impairment charge (new provisions): AED 240m Expected net loss (before provision release): AED 240m − 185m = AED 55m loss Window dressing: Management reviews Stage 2 book and "updates" ECL models — GCC economic outlook "improved" → Stage 2 provision rate revised down from 8% to 5.5% — Stage 2 book: AED 3,200m → provision release: 3,200 × (8%−5.5%) = AED 80m released Reported Q3 results: New impairment charge: 240m − 80m provision release = AED 160m Reported net profit: 185m − 160m = AED 25m ← now profitable! Reported cost of risk: 160m ÷ 20,000m × 4 = 3.2% annualised (down from 4.8% gross) Reality: The underlying credit book is no better. Stage 2 provision rates are now insufficient if conditions deteriorate further. The AED 80m will need to be re-raised in Q4 or Q5 — creating a "lumpy" provision pattern that is itself a detection signal.

    Footprint & Detection: Track net impairment charge = gross new provisions raised minus provision releases. A pattern of large provision raises in Q4 followed by releases in Q1–Q2 suggests P&L management. Calculate "through-the-cycle" cost of risk as a 3-year moving average — if quarterly cost of risk is highly volatile (0.3% one quarter, 3.5% the next) while the underlying credit quality appears stable, provision management is likely occurring. Also: compare Stage 2 ECL provision rates quarter-over-quarter. Sharp downward revisions in a deteriorating credit environment are a clear signal.

    4.13 — Detection
    🔍 Be Sherlock Holmes — Detecting Credit Quality Window Dressing
    "The game is afoot." — Six analytical tests that expose what the NPL ratio and broad coverage ratio are hiding.
    1

    The NPL / Stage 2 Divergence Clue: The most powerful single signal. Plot NPL ratio and Stage 2 ratio as percentage of gross loans on the same chart over 8 quarters. A flat or gently rising NPL ratio accompanied by a rapidly rising Stage 2 ratio is the primary signal of Stage 2 window dressing. The gap between the two lines represents the hidden stress — loans that should be NPL but are being kept current.

    Test: Stage 2 ratio trend over 8 quarters If Stage 2 rises >2% of gross loans per year while NPL is flat → flag Shadow NPL estimate: Stage 2 book × historical Stage 2→3 migration rate Example: Stage 2 = 21% of AED 20,000m = AED 4,200m Historical migration rate: 35% → shadow NPLs = AED 1,470m True NPL ratio estimate: (800 + 1,470) ÷ 20,000 = 11.35% vs reported 4.2% Hidden provision requirement: 1,470 × 45% LGD = AED 662m

    2

    The Narrow vs Broad Coverage Gap Clue: Calculate both narrow (specific provisions ÷ NPLs) and broad coverage independently. If the gap between them exceeds 30–35 percentage points, the bank is relying heavily on general provisions to present an attractive headline. Ask: if all Stage 2 general provisions were reclassified as specific (because Stage 2 migrates), what does narrow coverage become?

    Test: Broad − narrow coverage gap Comfortable: gap <25pp (specific provisions are the primary driver) Warning: gap 25–40pp (significant reliance on general provisions) Danger: gap >40pp (broad coverage is almost entirely general provisions) Secondary test: What is narrow coverage if we apply historical LGD? Correct specific provision = NPL × historical LGD% (not bank's assumption) Example: NPL AED 800m, bank uses 25% LGD → specific provision = AED 200m But historical LGD on UAE RE loans in stress = 45% Required provision = AED 360m → bank is under-provisioned by AED 160m

    3

    The Forbearance Ratio Clue: Request the forborne (modified/restructured) loan balance as a percentage of total loans. Forborne loans have 3–5× the probability of Stage 3 migration compared to non-forborne loans of similar profile. A large forbearance ratio — especially one growing in a deteriorating credit environment — signals that the bank is restructuring its way to a low NPL ratio without genuinely curing the underlying credit stress.

    Test: Forbearance ratio = forborne loans ÷ gross loans Benchmark: <3% forborne — normal; 3–7% — elevated; >7% — high concern For UAE RE-focused bank: benchmark against property cycle phase Forborne Stage 2 loans: must apply higher Stage 2 ECL provision rate Non-forborne Stage 2 provision rate: 5–8% Forborne Stage 2 provision rate should be: 12–18% (per EBA forborne classification guidance) If bank uses same rate for both → under-provisioning of forborne Stage 2 population

    4

    The Collateral Valuation Date Clue: Request the average vintage of collateral valuations by loan product type. Compare the reported portfolio LTV against the relevant UAE property price index movement since the last valuation date. A 12-month-old valuation in a market that has declined 18% represents significant hidden LTV deterioration.

    Test: LTV staleness indicator Request: Average date of last independent valuation by portfolio segment Cross-reference: ValuStrat / REIDIN / JLL UAE index change since valuation date Corrected LTV = Reported LTV ÷ (1 − property price decline since valuation) Example: Reported avg LTV 65%, valuations 18 months old, market fell 20% Corrected LTV: 65% ÷ (1 − 20%) = 65% ÷ 0.80 = 81.25% LGD implication: rises from ~15% to ~28% → stage 2 ECL model must reflect this

    5

    The Provision Release Pattern Clue: Track net impairment charge by quarter over 8 quarters. A "lumpy" pattern — large charges in some quarters, releases in others — while underlying credit quality appears stable is a provision management signal. Calculate the 8-quarter average cost of risk and compare to each individual quarter.

    Test: Provision volatility indicator Average 8-quarter cost of risk: e.g. 1.2% Standard deviation: if >0.5% → highly volatile → investigate each quarter of release Pattern red flag: releases always occur in Q1 (after Q4 large charges) or in quarters where recurring profit would otherwise show a loss Cross-check: Does Stage 2 provision rate decline in quarters where releases occur? If yes: ECL model assumptions are being used to manage quarterly earnings

    6

    The Write-Off Rate Clue: Compare write-off rate (amounts written off ÷ gross loans) to NPL inflow rate (new Stage 3 ÷ gross loans). If write-offs are running close to new NPL inflows, the bank is "cleaning" its book aggressively — keeping NPL ratio flat by removing old NPLs at the same rate new ones appear. This is legitimate if provisions are adequate. But if coverage was only 60–70% and write-offs are running at the NPL inflow rate, the bank is taking insufficient provisions then writing off faster than it recognises losses — creating a systematic under-provisioning cycle.

    Test: Write-off ratio = amounts written off ÷ opening NPL balance Benchmark: 15–30% annual write-off rate is normal (resolving aged NPLs) Red flag: >40% write-off rate with narrow coverage <70% → Bank is writing off before fully provisioning → future P&L charge certain Cross-check: Recovery rate on written-off loans after 12 months A bank writing off at 100% provision then recovering 30% is correctly provisioned A bank writing off at 60% provision then recovering 5% was 35% under-provisioned
